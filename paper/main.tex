\begin{abstract}
AI-powered coding agents (Claude Code, OpenAI Codex, GitHub Copilot, Cursor, and Devin) now contribute at scale to open-source repositories through pull requests. When these branches incorporate upstream changes via merge commits, textual conflicts arise and must be resolved by the agent or a human. We present a large-scale empirical study of merge conflicts in AI-authored PRs, covering approximately 930{,}000 PRs across 116{,}000 repositories from the AIDev catalogue. For each PR we identify internal merge commits, reproduce them with \texttt{diff3}, localize each resolution, and classify it into one of seven strategies following \citet{ghiotto2020nature}. Our pipeline recovers $50{,}700$ distinct merges across $210{,}902$ PRs in $57{,}582$ repositories; $29.5\%$ produce at least one conflict, yielding $121{,}599$ chunks. Among classifiable chunks, $73.7\%$ adopt one side verbatim (V1~$49.1\%$, V2~$24.6\%$), matching the $75\%$ reported for human Java merges; agents introduce new code less often ($10.6\%$ vs.\ $13\%$). Conflict incidence varies more than $4{\times}$ across agents, from Copilot ($10.5\%$) to Cursor ($47.8\%$), and most conflicting merges are structurally small (median~2 chunks). The resolver is the agent itself in just $5.4\%$ of merges, ranging from $1.2\%$ (Copilot) to $24.3\%$ (Devin), and a human in $94.3\%$. When agents resolve autonomously, they select~V1 in $79.7\%$ of chunks versus $45.2\%$ for humans (Cram\'{e}r's~$V{=}0.23$, moderate effect), with almost no new-code resolutions ($3.9\%$ vs.\ $11.5\%$). These findings establish the first empirical baseline for merge-conflict behavior in agentic AI and reveal a sharp divide between human and agent resolution strategies.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

The increasing adoption of AI-powered coding agents, such as Claude Code, OpenAI Codex, GitHub Copilot, Cursor, and Devin, represents a paradigm shift in software development workflows. These agents autonomously create branches, implement features, and open pull requests (PRs) in open-source repositories, contributing code alongside human developers at a scale that only became possible in the last two years. The AIDev catalogue~\cite{liaidev2026} alone documents over 930{,}000 AI-authored PRs across 116{,}000 repositories, positioning agentic contributions as a first-class citizen of modern collaborative development.

When an AI-authored branch needs to incorporate upstream changes (e.g., via \texttt{git merge main}), textual merge conflicts may arise. These conflicts must be resolved before the merged code can be committed, and the resolution may be performed by the AI agent itself, by a human developer, or collaboratively. Despite the scale of agentic contributions, the nature of the merge conflicts produced inside AI-authored PRs, how large they are, which strategies are applied to resolve them, and who actually performs the resolution, remains largely unexplored, leaving fundamental questions unanswered about a workflow that is already used in hundreds of thousands of real-world merges.

Prior work on merge conflict resolution has focused exclusively on human developers~\cite{ghiotto2020nature,vale2022challenges,dinella2022deepmerge,svyatkovskiy2022mergebert,dong2023mergegen}: the canonical characterizations, taxonomies, and automated resolvers were all mined, trained, and evaluated on human-authored merges, and thus reveal nothing about how conflicts behave when one of the parents comes from an autonomous agent. The one dataset that targets agentic PRs, AgenticFlict~\cite{agenticflict2025}, identifies conflicts by \emph{simulating} a two-way merge between the PR head and its base branch, but it neither observes the real merge commits performed in the wild nor captures how conflicts were actually resolved, and it does not distinguish AI from human actors in the integration step. Consequently, no prior study has characterized the structural nature of conflicts in AI-authored branches, the resolution strategies applied to them, or the distribution of resolver identities on real merge commits.

In this paper, we address these gaps by conducting a large-scale empirical study on real internal merge commits within AI-authored pull requests from the full AIDev catalogue. We reproduce each merge with \texttt{diff3}, localize the accepted resolution for every conflicting chunk following \citet{dinella2022deepmerge}, classify each resolution using the Ghiotto et al.~\cite{ghiotto2020nature} taxonomy, and attribute each resolution to an AI agent or a human based on the committer identity. Our main contributions are: (i)~the first characterization of the structural nature of merge conflicts in AI-authored branches at scale; (ii)~a comparison of resolution strategies against the human Java baseline of \citet{ghiotto2020nature}; (iii)~an analysis of who resolves these conflicts and how the strategy profile differs by resolver; and (iv)~an open replication package containing all collection and analysis scripts. Concretely, we answer the following research questions:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{RQ1: What is the structural nature of merge conflicts in AI-authored branches---are they typically small and tractable, or large and complex?} Understanding this is fundamental for assessing whether automated resolution is feasible at scale. We characterize the distributions of the number of conflicting chunks per merge and the size (in lines of code) of each conflict side and its resolution.
    \item \textbf{RQ2: Which resolution strategies are applied to these conflicts, and how does their distribution compare to the human baseline?} Knowing which strategies agents and humans apply informs the design of automated resolvers and reveals how agentic contributions differ from human ones. We measure how resolution decisions are distributed across the seven strategy categories and compare the distribution against the human Java baseline of \citet{ghiotto2020nature}.
    \item \textbf{RQ3: Who resolves the conflicts introduced in AI-authored PRs, and how does resolver identity correlate with resolution strategy?} Whether conflicts are resolved by the agent autonomously or by a human has direct implications for pipeline design and trust in agentic workflows. We identify who commits each internal merge and how resolver identity correlates with the strategy chosen.
\end{itemize}

Our results show that merge conflicts in agent-authored branches are structurally small (median of two chunks of 2--3 LOC) but exhibit a heavy right tail that concentrates most of the workload in a small fraction of merges, and that conflict incidence varies by more than $4{\times}$ across agents (Copilot $10.5\%$ to Cursor $47.8\%$). Among classifiable chunks, $73.7\%$ adopt one side verbatim, essentially matching the $75\%$ reported by \citet{ghiotto2020nature} for human Java merges, while agents introduce new code less often ($10.6\%$ vs.\ $13\%$). Integration, however, is overwhelmingly human-led: $94.3\%$ of internal merges are committed by a human, and when agents do resolve autonomously, they concentrate $79.7\%$ of decisions on~$V_1$, far exceeding the human rate of $45.2\%$ (Cram\'{e}r's~$V{=}0.23$), a gap that persists across all sensitivity scenarios we evaluated.

The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. Section~\ref{sec:background} presents background and related work. Section~\ref{sec:methodology} describes our methodology and pipeline. Section~\ref{sec:results} reports results for each RQ. Section~\ref{sec:discussion} discusses implications. Section~\ref{sec:threats} addresses threats to validity, and Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes.

\section{Background and Related Work}
\label{sec:background}

\subsection{Software Merging and Merge Conflicts}
\label{sec:bg-merging}

Collaborative software development relies on branching, whereby developers work in parallel on independent copies of a code base and later reconcile their contributions via \emph{merging}~\cite{mens2002survey}. Unstructured three-way merge, as implemented by \texttt{git merge}, compares each pair of versions against their common ancestor and automatically combines non-overlapping edits~\cite{mens2002survey,ghiotto2020nature,cavalcanti2019}. When both sides edit the same textual region, the merger reports a \emph{conflict} and delegates resolution to the developer. Git records conflicting regions with the familiar \verb|<<<<<<<|, \verb|=======|, and \verb|>>>>>>>| markers, and the \texttt{diff3} output additionally preserves the common ancestor between \verb+|||||||+ and \verb|=======|, yielding the three-way view on which subsequent analyses depend~\cite{ghiotto2020nature,dinella2022deepmerge}.

Merge conflicts are frequent and costly. \citet{ghiotto2020nature} inspected 960{,}366 merge scenarios across 2{,}731 open-source Java projects and found that 11.9\% produced at least one conflict, yielding 175{,}805 conflicting chunks. Similar rates (10--20\%) have been reported across ecosystems~\cite{ghiotto2020nature,vale2022challenges,lessenich2018indicators}. \citet{vale2022challenges} combined the mining of roughly 81{,}000 merge scenarios from 66 projects with a developer survey and identified four recurring challenges: understanding the conflict, making the correct resolution decision, dealing with tooling, and coordinating with teammates. \citet{lessenich2018indicators} combined a developer survey with a mining study of 163 projects and showed that the indicators developers \emph{believe} predict conflicts correlate only weakly with measured conflict rates, motivating data-driven investigations. \citet{nelson2019lifecycle} complemented these quantitative findings with a qualitative life-cycle study, documenting the understand--plan--resolve--verify workflow and the heuristics developers employ (e.g., prefer the newer change, prefer the one they authored). Resolution time correlates with the number, size, and structural complexity of conflicting chunks, as well as with the familiarity of the developer with the affected code.

Beyond line-based merging, semistructured and structured approaches~\cite{apel2011semistructured,cavalcanti2019,accioly2018semistructured} reduce spurious conflicts by parsing artifacts at coarser granularities, and proactive tools~\cite{brun2011proactive,kasi2013cassandra} warn developers before conflicts materialize. \citet{boll2024semi} showed that 87.9\% of conflicting chunks follow a small, language-agnostic catalogue of resolution patterns, suggesting that semi-automation is more feasible than previously assumed. These studies collectively motivate systematic, data-driven investigations of how conflicts are actually resolved, the gap this paper addresses for AI-authored pull requests.

\subsection{Merge Conflict Resolution Strategies}
\label{sec:bg-strategies}

\paragraph{Taxonomy of resolutions.}
\citet{ghiotto2020nature} introduced the canonical taxonomy that classifies each resolved chunk by comparing the merged text against the two conflicting versions ($v_1$, $v_2$): the developer may adopt one version verbatim (\textit{V1}, \textit{V2}), concatenate both sides in either order (\textit{ConcatV1V2}, \textit{ConcatV2V1}), interleave tokens or lines from both (\textit{Combination}), write content absent from both sides (\textit{New Code}), or resolve to an empty region (\textit{None}). An additional \textit{Imprecise} label is used when the resolution cannot be reliably mapped to the conflict region (e.g., because the file was substantially reorganized). Across the 175{,}805 Java chunks they studied, \textit{V1} and \textit{V2} together account for 57\% of resolutions, \textit{New Code} for 22\%, and concatenation/combination for 14\%, with \textit{None} (6.7\%) covering trivial or empty resolutions. We adopt this taxonomy, collapsing \textit{ConcatV1V2} and \textit{ConcatV2V1} into a single \textit{CC} label and folding \textit{Combination} into a \textit{CB} class, which gives us six classifiable labels (V1, V2, CC, CB, NC, NN) plus \textit{Imprecise}, directly comparable to the human baseline.

\paragraph{Automated resolution: classification and generation.}
Machine-learning-based resolvers have matured along two paradigms. The \emph{classification} paradigm (\textsc{DeepMerge}~\cite{dinella2022deepmerge}, \textsc{MergeBERT}~\cite{svyatkovskiy2022mergebert}) trains models to select a strategy label; \textsc{MergeBERT} reaches 63--68\% accuracy on a five-class output space. The \emph{generation} paradigm (\textsc{MergeGen}~\cite{dong2023mergegen}, \textsc{ChatMerge}~\cite{shen2023chatmerge}) synthesizes resolution text directly, outperforming classifiers and capturing \textit{New Code} resolutions that label-pickers cannot emit. Search-based approaches (\textsc{SBCR}~\cite{camposjunior2024sbcr}, \citet{camposjunior2022sbes}) reconstruct resolutions from lines already present in the conflicting versions, achieving up to a 94.7\% reduction in the effective search space. Further specialized methods include template-based synthesis~\cite{pan2021synthesis}, data-mining recommenders~\cite{elias2023mestre}, and per-project three-way classifiers~\cite{aldndni2023rpredictor}.

\paragraph{Prediction and benchmarks.}
Complementary to resolution, conflict \emph{prediction} aims to warn developers early. \citet{owhadikareshk2019} trained a lightweight classifier on 267{,}657 merge scenarios across seven languages and reported F1 above 0.80 on most projects, without running the full merge. \citet{brindescu2020planning} pushed the prediction target further, using chunk-level features (size, construct, number of edit locations) to estimate the \emph{time} a developer will spend on a conflict, which informs our choice of structural features for RQ1. \textsc{ConflictBench}~\cite{shenmeng2024conflictbench} curates 180 Java scenarios with ground-truth resolutions to standardize the evaluation of merge tools, addressing the reproducibility gap left by project-specific mining studies.

\paragraph{Gap.}
All the studies above treat \emph{humans} as the implicit resolvers. No prior work has characterized the structural nature or the resolution strategies of conflicts in AI-authored branches, nor has any study separated AI-performed from human-performed resolutions in such branches. Our work fills that gap by reusing Ghiotto's structural metrics and strategy taxonomy on a population of merges that only became possible after the recent rise of autonomous coding agents.

\subsection{AI Agents in Software Development}
\label{sec:bg-agents}

The past two years have seen the emergence of autonomous coding agents that go beyond code completion and act as first-class contributors on GitHub. \citet{liaidev2025se3} label this regime \emph{Software Engineering 3.0}, in which agents independently open branches, write code, invoke tests, and submit pull requests that are subsequently reviewed by humans or by other agents. The rise of these agents is anchored in strong quantitative evidence: \citet{peng2023copilot} ran a randomized controlled trial of GitHub Copilot with 95 developers and observed a 55\% speed-up on a realistic programming task, establishing that AI coding assistance materially alters developer workflow. \citet{wang2024llmagents} survey the architectures of LLM-based software-engineering agents (planner, actor, reviewer patterns) and the benchmarks used to evaluate them, most prominently \textsc{SWE-bench}~\cite{jimenez2024swebench}, which requires agents to synthesize a patch that passes the original test suite for a real GitHub issue. Although frontier agents still solve only a minority of \textsc{SWE-bench} instances, the benchmark has become the reference point for measuring progress on real-world coding. The present study targets five representative agents: Anthropic's \textbf{Claude Code}, OpenAI's \textbf{Codex}, GitHub's \textbf{Copilot} (in its agentic mode), \textbf{Cursor} (the Cursor Agent), and Cognition's \textbf{Devin}. Each agent authors commits under a distinctive bot identity (e.g., \texttt{claude[bot]}, \texttt{openai-codex[bot]}, \texttt{copilot[bot]}, \texttt{cursor-agent[bot]}, \texttt{devin-ai-integration[bot]}), which is what makes it possible to attribute branches and resolutions to agents at scale.

Empirical studies of these agents remain scarce. \citet{liaidev2025se3,liaidev2026} characterize \emph{agentic} PRs along dimensions such as acceptance rate, PR size, review turnaround, and topic distribution, using the AIDev dataset (see Section~\ref{sec:bg-agenticflict}). They do not, however, examine how branches are integrated: in particular, whether agents create merge commits, how often those merges conflict, and who resolves the conflicts when they do. \citet{agenticflict2025} make an initial move in that direction by \emph{simulating} a two-way merge between the PR's head and base commits to count conflicts, but their analysis does not examine the actual merge commits on the branch or how those conflicts were resolved. Our work is also informed by earlier studies of bot-authored PRs, most notably \citet{alfadel2023dependabot}, who mined Dependabot security PRs at scale and established the methodology for studying bot contributions on GitHub. Modern coding agents differ from rule-based bots such as Dependabot in that they synthesize open-ended code rather than apply fixed templates, which is precisely why their conflict-resolution behavior warrants a dedicated study. Our study complements this work by analyzing real merge commits within the PR branch, distinguishing AI-driven from human-driven resolutions, and applying the human-centric resolution taxonomy to this new population.

\subsection{The AIDev and AgenticFlict Datasets}
\label{sec:bg-agenticflict}

Two datasets currently underpin empirical research on AI coding agents. \textbf{AIDev}~\cite{liaidev2026} is a large-scale catalogue of agent-authored pull requests mined from the GitHub Archive: it covers 932{,}791 PRs opened by five agents (Claude Code, Codex, Copilot, Cursor, Devin) across 116{,}211 repositories and 72{,}631 developers. AIDev provides the full PR metadata (title, body, diff summary, state, \texttt{base\_oid}, \texttt{head\_oid}, \texttt{merged\_at}) but no conflict information. Throughout this paper we use the two identifiers \texttt{base\_oid} and \texttt{head\_oid} in the sense of the GitHub REST API~\cite{liaidev2026}: given a pull request, \texttt{head\_oid} is the 40-character SHA-1 of the tip commit of the PR's \emph{source} (feature) branch, i.e., the last commit the agent authored, while \texttt{base\_oid} is the SHA-1 of the tip commit of the target branch (typically \texttt{main} or \texttt{master}) at the moment the PR was opened. Together they delimit the range of commits the PR proposes to land. In Git notation, the commits introduced by the PR are exactly \verb|base_oid..head_oid|, and a three-way merge of the PR with its target branch uses \texttt{base\_oid} and \texttt{head\_oid} as the two parent revisions.

\textbf{AgenticFlict}~\cite{agenticflict2025} is built on top of AIDev and adds a first conflict layer: by locally cloning each repository and running a two-way \texttt{git merge} between \texttt{base\_oid} and \texttt{head\_oid}, the authors identify 29{,}609 conflicting PRs among 142{,}652 PRs in 59{,}412 repositories (a 27.67\% simulated-conflict rate), yielding 336{,}380 conflict regions. AgenticFlict's key contribution is demonstrating that conflicts in agent-authored code are frequent and non-trivial; however, the dataset has two limitations that motivate our work. First, the conflicts it reports are \emph{simulated}: they represent the merge conditions at the time of PR closure (for closed but unmerged PRs) or data collection (for open PRs), but they do not correspond to any real merge commit performed in the wild. Consequently, the dataset contains no \emph{resolutions}: there is no record of how conflicts were actually handled, because no merge was ever committed. Second, AgenticFlict does not distinguish AI from human actors in the merging process, since no merge actor exists in its simulated setup. Our study addresses both gaps directly from AIDev: we look \emph{inside} each PR branch for real two-parent commits (``internal merges''), reproduce those merges with \texttt{diff3}, localize the accepted resolution for every conflicting chunk in the resulting merge commit, and attribute each resolution to the author of the merge, classifying them as AI or human based on the committer identity.

\subsection{PR Selection and Scope}
\label{sec:meth-selection}

Our study is scoped to the full AIDev catalogue~\cite{liaidev2026}, which documents approximately 930{,}000 pull requests opened by five AI agents (Claude Code, OpenAI Codex, GitHub Copilot, Cursor, and Devin) across roughly 116{,}000 repositories. We consider PRs in all states (\textit{merged}, \textit{closed without merge}, and \textit{open}): restricting to merged PRs would bias the sample toward PRs whose history was rewritten via rebase- or squash-and-merge and would drop exactly the intermediate merge commits we want to study. The per-PR commit inventory needed to locate those internal merges is not shipped with the full AIDev tier; we therefore produce it ourselves by fetching \texttt{refs/pull/*/head} for every in-scope repository and enumerating, per PR, the commits between the fork-point with the base branch and the PR tip.

Applying this procedure to the full AIDev catalogue yields an in-scope dataset of $210{,}902$ pull requests across $57{,}582$ distinct repositories. Our commit-chronology extractor (Pass A) could not produce a complete SHA list for $25{,}571$ of these PRs ($12.1\%$): $20{,}439$ shared no common ancestor with the base branch, indicating entirely disconnected histories (e.g., orphan branches or repositories whose default branch was replaced); $5{,}132$ had their PR head reference no longer accessible at cloning time; and $735$ raised other exceptions. These PRs contribute no commit data and therefore no internal merge commits to our analysis. Table~\ref{tab:scope-per-agent} reports the per-agent distribution: as in AIDev itself, OpenAI Codex dominates the dataset (approximately 76\% of in-scope PRs), with GitHub Copilot, Cursor, Devin, and Claude Code covering the long tail.

\subsection{Identifying Merge Commits Within PR Branches}
\label{sec:meth-merges}

For each PR, we identify merge commits (commits with exactly two parents) from the set of SHAs produced by the chronology step. These are merges that the PR author (or collaborators) created to bring upstream changes into the feature branch, typically via \texttt{git merge main}, and they are distinct from the single ``integration merge'' that GitHub creates when a PR is accepted. We exclude octopus merges (three or more parents), following \citet{ghiotto2020nature}, and the GitHub integration merge itself: any two-parent commit whose subject matches \texttt{\textasciicircum{}Merge pull request \#$d$+} (case-insensitive) is filtered out, because GitHub's own pre-merge check gates the PR and makes those merges almost always conflict-free. Across the full in-scope dataset, this step excluded $70{,}042$ unique integration-merge SHAs (spanning $31{,}793$ PRs) and $93$ octopus-merge SHAs (spanning $581$ PRs).

\paragraph{Merge-level deduplication.} A single physical merge commit can appear on the head branches of several PRs (force-push, re-open, cherry-pick), so a naive per-PR indexing overcounts the same physical artefact. All merge- and chunk-level statistics in this paper are therefore computed on natural keys that discard the PR identifier: a two-field key (repository, merge-commit SHA) for merges and a four-field key (repository, merge SHA, file path, chunk index) for chunks.

Applying these filters to the in-scope dataset returns $452{,}114$ raw PR-level merge references, which collapse to $50{,}700$ distinct physical merge commits after deduplication, an inflation factor of $8.92\times$ between the raw PR-level indexing and the true physical artefact. In fact, $54.4\%$ of physical merge commits are referenced by two or more PRs, and the most extreme case is a single merge that appears on the head branches of $848$ distinct PRs. A total of $12{,}299$ PRs ($5.8\%$ of the in-scope dataset) contribute at least one internal merge; the remaining $94.2\%$ land as fast-forwards, as squash-merges, or are closed without merging, and therefore do not expose a merge commit inside the PR's branch history.

\subsection{Reproducing Merges and Extracting Conflict Chunks}
\label{sec:meth-conflicts}

For each merge commit with parents $p_1$ and $p_2$, we compute the merge base ($b = \text{merge-base}(p_1, p_2)$) and identify files modified on both sides using \texttt{git merge-tree}. For each such file, we reproduce the three-way merge using:

\begin{verbatim}
git merge-file -p --diff3 p1_file base_file p2_file
\end{verbatim}

We parse the resulting diff3 output to extract individual conflict chunks, each delimited by \verb|<<<<<<<|, \verb+|||||||+ , \verb|=======|, and \verb|>>>>>>>| markers. Each chunk produces three text regions: \textit{v1} (from $p_1$), \textit{base} (from $b$), and \textit{v2} (from $p_2$). For each chunk we also record the non-blank line count of each side and of the resolved text, computed after trimming whitespace, consistent with Ghiotto~et~al.'s~\cite{ghiotto2020nature} normalization. The resolution size is the metric this study adds relative to their characterization.

\subsection{Localizing the Resolution}
\label{sec:meth-resolution}

For each conflict chunk, we retrieve the resolved file from the merge commit and apply the \textsc{LocalizeResRegion} algorithm of \citet{dinella2022deepmerge}, which isolates the specific resolution text by anchoring on the unique textual context (prefix and suffix) surrounding the conflict region in the three-way merge output. If unique anchors cannot be found, the chunk is marked \emph{Imprecise}. \citet{dinella2022deepmerge} validated the algorithm on 174 conflict regions from 50 randomly selected merges and found 100\% correct localization (95\%~CI: $[0.979, 1.0]$); we use it on this basis.

A more subtle threat arises from the \emph{differential Imprecise rate} between resolver types: the combined Imprecise rate is $16.7\%$ for agent-resolved chunks versus $40.0\%$ for human-resolved chunks. This gap has two distinct components. The first is the \emph{localization failure} component: agent-resolved chunks fail to anchor in $16.1\%$ of cases versus $33.4\%$ for human-resolved chunks, a differential likely driven by the agent's high $V_1$ rate---$V_1$ resolutions precisely match one parent and are therefore easier for \textsc{LocalizeResRegion} to anchor. The agent's $V_1$ preference and its lower localization failure rate are therefore mutually reinforcing. The second component is \emph{Postponed}: cases where localization succeeded but the resolved file still contained conflict markers; these account for $0.64\%$ of agent-resolved chunks versus $6.56\%$ of human-resolved chunks (Section~\ref{sec:rq2}), contributing an additional $\approx$6~pp to the Imprecise differential. We cannot fully disentangle the localization-failure component from the strategy distribution; the sensitivity analysis in Section~\ref{sec:rq3} bounds the agent-to-human $V_1$ ratio across all Imprecise reassignment scenarios. % An upper-bound sensitivity analysis (treating all Imprecise chunks as $V_1$) and a lower-bound analysis (treating them as NC) are provided in the supplementary material.

\subsection{Classifying Resolution Strategies}
\label{sec:meth-strategies}

We classify each resolved chunk into one of seven resolution strategies, following the taxonomy of \citet{ghiotto2020nature}, by comparing the normalized resolution text against both conflict sides:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{V1} / \textbf{V2}: The resolution is identical to one side of the conflict.
    \item \textbf{CC} (\emph{Concatenation}): The resolution is the concatenation of both sides in either order (we fold both orderings into \textit{CC} to stay comparable with \citet{ghiotto2020nature}).
    \item \textbf{CB} (\emph{Combination}): The resolution interleaves lines from both sides without introducing new lines.
    \item \textbf{NC} (\emph{New Code}): The resolution introduces content not present in either side.
    \item \textbf{NN} (\emph{None}): The resolution is empty.
    \item \textbf{Imprecise}: The resolution could not be reliably isolated (Section~\ref{sec:meth-resolution}) and is kept as a separate bucket to avoid silent misclassification.
\end{itemize}

Because \citet{ghiotto2020nature} do not report an \textit{Imprecise} bucket, RQ2 distributions are reported over the six classifiable labels, with the \textit{Imprecise} share stated separately.

\subsection{Identifying the Resolver}
\label{sec:meth-resolver}

We determine who resolved each conflict by analyzing the committer metadata of the merge commit. We classify the resolver as \textit{AI}, \textit{Human}, or \textit{Agent-assisted} based on the bot identities described in Section~\ref{sec:bg-agents}, following the same account-to-agent mapping used by \citet{liaidev2025se3}.

\subsection{Analyses per Research Question}
\label{sec:meth-analyses}

\textbf{RQ1: Structural nature.} We report the distribution of the number of conflicting chunks per merge and the size of each conflict side and resolution (as described in Section~\ref{sec:meth-conflicts}), globally and per cross-cutting stratum (agent, language, PR task type), with summary statistics (median, mean, standard deviation, and key percentiles). Visualizations follow \citet{ghiotto2020nature} to enable direct visual comparison.

\textbf{RQ2: Resolution strategies.} We report the relative frequency of each resolution label (V1, V2, CC, CB, NC, NN) at the chunk level, globally and per stratum, with the \textit{Imprecise} share reported separately (Section~\ref{sec:meth-strategies}). The comparison with \citet{ghiotto2020nature} is numerical (no empirical human dataset is collected in this study).

\textbf{RQ3: Resolver.} We report the distribution of resolver type (agent-resolved, human-resolved) at the merge-commit level, globally and per stratum.

\paragraph{Statistical analyses.}
All proportions are reported with 95\% Wilson (score) confidence intervals~\cite{wilson1927probable}, which handle small counts better than the normal approximation. For distributional comparisons across agents (RQ1), we use the non-parametric Kruskal-Wallis test followed by post-hoc Dunn tests with Bonferroni correction~\cite{dunn1964multiple}; effect sizes are reported as Cliff's~$\delta$~\cite{cliff1993dominance} since the distributions are heavily right-skewed. For association between categorical variables (RQ2, RQ3), we use chi-squared tests of independence; all chi-squared results are accompanied by the bias-corrected Cram\'{e}r's~$V$~\cite{bergsma2013bias} as the primary effect-size metric ($V{<}0.10$: weak; $0.10$--$0.30$: moderate; ${\geq}0.30$: strong). We report~$V$ rather than~$p$ as the primary summary because at $n{\approx}60{,}000$ chunks virtually any difference yields $p{<}0.001$; magnitude therefore carries more information than statistical significance. A validity caveat applies to all tests: conflict chunks within the same merge commit and across commits within the same repository are not fully independent, so all~$p$-values are anti-conservative (i.e., smaller than they would be under strict independence). All reported~$V$ values are therefore treated as \emph{upper bounds} on the true population effect. Because a goodness-of-fit test against \citet{ghiotto2020nature} is inappropriate given different populations, within-cluster dependence, and power inflation at this sample size, we instead report the absolute percentage-point difference per strategy and whether Ghiotto~et~al.'s estimate falls within our 95\% Wilson CI.

\subsection{Dataset Overview}
\label{sec:res-overview}

Our pipeline covers the full AIDev catalogue and recovers, after merge-level deduplication, $50{,}700$ distinct internal merge commits authored inside the $210{,}902$ in-scope PRs across $57{,}582$ repositories (Table~\ref{tab:dataset-overview}). Of those merges, $14{,}960$ ($29.5\%$) produce at least one textual conflict, yielding $121{,}599$ conflict chunks. At the PR level, $12{,}299$ PRs ($5.8\%$) contribute at least one internal merge and $7{,}268$ ($3.4\%$) contain at least one conflicting merge, confirming that internal merges are the exception rather than the rule in agent-authored branches and that conflicts, when they do arise, cluster inside a comparatively small slice of the dataset. The resolution-localization algorithm of \citet{dinella2022deepmerge} uniquely identifies the resolution region for $83{,}043$ of the $121{,}599$ chunks ($68.3\%$); the remaining $45{,}847$ ($37.7\%$) are kept as \emph{Imprecise} and reported separately throughout the paper. This bucket has two distinct origins: $38{,}556$ chunks ($31.7\%$) for which the algorithm could not find unique context anchors around the conflict region, and $7{,}291$ chunks ($6.0\%$) classified as \emph{Postponed}---cases where localization succeeded but the committed file still contained unresolved conflict markers (\verb|<<<<<<<|, \verb|=======|, \verb|>>>>>>>|), indicating that the conflict was committed without being resolved. Both are folded into Imprecise to avoid silent misclassification; the Postponed sub-category is analyzed further in Sections~\ref{sec:rq2} and~\ref{sec:disc-tools}.

\subsection{RQ1: Structural Nature}
\label{sec:rq1}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figures/rq2_size_distributions}
  \caption{Size distributions in agent-authored merges (outliers omitted for clarity). \textit{Left:} number of conflicting chunks per merge commit (conflicting merges only, $n{=}14{,}960$). \textit{Right:} non-blank LOC for the $v_1$, $v_2$, and resolution sides of the $121{,}599$ conflict chunks.}
  \Description{Two side-by-side boxplots. Left shows the distribution of conflicting chunks per merge. Right shows three side-by-side boxplots for v1, v2, and resolution LOC.}
  \label{fig:size-distributions}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Chunks per merge.} Figure~\ref{fig:size-distributions} (left) summarises the structural size distributions. Across the $14{,}960$ conflicting merge commits, the number of conflicting chunks is heavily right-skewed: the median is $2$, the 75th percentile is $4$, and the 90th percentile is $10$, yet the mean is $8.13$ (standard deviation $93.6$) because the tail reaches $9{,}791$ chunks in a single merge. Three quarters of conflicting merges therefore contain at most four chunks and would be individually tractable, while a long tail of merges, concentrated above the 99th percentile ($121$ chunks), are arbitrarily complex. Restricting the distribution to all $50{,}700$ internal merges, including conflict-free ones, drops the median to $0$ and the 90th percentile to $3$, confirming that most agent-authored internal merges integrate cleanly.

\paragraph{Side sizes.} Chunk-level sizes on the $v_1$ and $v_2$ sides (Figure~\ref{fig:size-distributions}, right) mirror each other closely: the median size is $3$ LOC for $v_1$ and $2$ LOC for $v_2$ after whitespace-only normalization, with 75th percentiles of $12$ and $6$ and 99th percentiles of $275$ and $150$ respectively. The largest chunk side size is $199{,}765$ lines, an outlier arising from a bulk-refactor generated by Codex on a large monorepo.

\paragraph{Resolution size.} The resolution-size distribution, the metric this study adds relative to \citet{ghiotto2020nature}, shows a similar skew (Figure~\ref{fig:size-distributions}, right, green boxplot). The median resolution is $1$ LOC long and the 75th percentile is $6$, closely tracking the median of $v_1$. The tail, however, is heavier: the 99th percentile reaches $1{,}407$ LOC and the maximum $199{,}769$, slightly larger than the largest $v_1$ seen in the sample. This pattern is compatible with a dominant ``pick a side'' behaviour (Section~\ref{sec:rq2}) combined with occasional rewrites that introduce material new content alongside the resolution.

\paragraph{Per-stratum incidence.} Conflict incidence at the PR level varies by agent: Claude Code leads with $8.2\%$ of its $1{,}888$ PRs producing at least one conflict, followed by OpenAI Codex ($3.6\%$ of $160{,}832$), Devin ($3.3\%$ of $11{,}433$), Copilot ($3.0\%$ of $21{,}846$), and Cursor ($2.1\%$ of $14{,}903$). At the merge-commit level the ranking reorders because agents differ in how often they create internal merges at all: Cursor ($47.8\%$ conflict rate among its $1{,}013$ internal merges) and OpenAI Codex ($39.9\%$ of $29{,}250$) are the most conflict-prone, whereas Copilot's $17{,}216$ merges yield conflicts in only $10.5\%$ of cases, consistent with Copilot's use of comparatively short-lived feature branches. Rust and JavaScript exhibit the highest per-language conflict incidence at the PR level ($4.9\%$ and $4.7\%$ respectively), while Go repositories sit below $1.3\%$.


\paragraph{Statistical tests.} A Kruskal-Wallis test on the number of conflict chunks per conflicting merge across agents yields $H{=}92.1$ ($p{<}0.001$), indicating that at least one agent differs. Post-hoc Dunn tests (Bonferroni-corrected) reveal that OpenAI Codex differs significantly from Claude Code ($p{<}0.001$), Cursor ($p{<}0.001$), and Devin ($p{<}0.001$); Copilot differs significantly from both Claude Code ($p{=}0.001$) and Cursor ($p{<}0.001$); all consistent with Codex's and Copilot's smaller median chunk count ($2$) compared to Claude Code and Cursor (median $3$). Effect sizes are uniformly negligible to small (Cliff's~$\delta{<}0.17$ for all pairs), confirming that per-agent differences in the number of chunks per merge, although detectable, are not practically large. Side and resolution LOC distributions differ significantly across agents (Kruskal-Wallis $H{=}97.8$--$2414$, all $p{<}0.001$); interpreting these differences requires controlling for the programming languages targeted by each agent, which differ substantially across the dataset.

\textbf{Summary.} Structurally, merge conflicts in agent-authored branches are \emph{small and common}, with a \emph{long heavy tail}: most conflicting merges contain only a handful of short chunks, but a non-trivial minority (above the 99th percentile) are arbitrarily complex. The median chunk in our population is on the order of one to three lines on each side, and the resolution is of comparable size, placing our observations near the short-chunk end of the \citet{ghiotto2020nature} distribution for human Java merges. Per-agent conflict rates at the merge-commit level span more than $4\times$, from Copilot ($10.5\%$, 95\%~CI $[10.1\%,11.0\%]$) to Cursor ($47.8\%$, $[44.7\%,50.9\%]$), underscoring that any tooling informed by conflict prevalence needs to be interpreted against the agent that produced the branch.

\begin{findingbox}
\textbf{Finding 1.} Conflicts in agent-authored merges are structurally small: the median conflicting merge contains~2 chunks, each spanning 2--3 lines per side (Figure~\ref{fig:size-distributions}). The distribution is heavily right-skewed; a small minority of merges above the 99th percentile concentrates most of the conflict workload. \\[3pt]
\textbf{Finding 2.} Conflict incidence at the merge-commit level varies by more than $4\times$ across agents, from~$10.5\%$ (Copilot) to~$47.8\%$ (Cursor), reflecting differences in branching strategy rather than code quality alone.
\end{findingbox}

\subsection{RQ2: Resolution Strategies}
\label{sec:rq2}

\paragraph{Global distribution.} Over the $121{,}599$ conflict chunks, $37.7\%$ are marked \emph{Imprecise} by the localization step and are reported separately (Section~\ref{sec:meth-strategies}); the remaining $75{,}752$ classifiable chunks are the basis of the numbers below and of Figure~\ref{fig:rq2-global-ghiotto}. Among classifiable chunks, \textbf{$73.7\%$ of resolutions take one side verbatim} ($V_1$ $49.1\%$, $V_2$ $24.6\%$), $15.1\%$ mix the two sides (\emph{CC} $6.2\%$, \emph{CB} $9.0\%$), $10.6\%$ introduce new code not present in either parent (\emph{NC}), and $0.6\%$ resolve to an empty region (\emph{NN}). For direct comparison, \citet{ghiotto2020nature} report that humans resolving Java conflicts pick a single side in $75\%$ of chunks, mix sides in $12\%$, and write new code in $13\%$. Agents are therefore \emph{comparable to humans in picking one side verbatim} ($73.7\%$ vs.\ $75\%$), \emph{slightly less likely to introduce new code} ($10.6\%$ vs.\ $13\%$), and \emph{slightly more likely to mix} ($15.1\%$ vs.\ $12\%$). Taken together, agent-authored conflicts are resolved with a \textbf{conservative playbook}.

\paragraph{Per-agent breakdown.} The $V_1$ bias is strongest in Copilot (excl.\ Imprecise: $V_1$ $54.1\%$, $V_2$ $21.5\%$) and OpenAI Codex ($V_1$ $50.7\%$, $V_2$ $22.7\%$), both of which drive the global distribution by volume. Claude Code and Devin invert the polarity, favouring $V_2$ ($36.5\%$ and $47.3\%$ respectively against $V_1$ at $41.7\%$ and $27.6\%$); Cursor sits in between ($V_1$ $36.5\%$, $V_2$ $30.5\%$). The \emph{NC} (new-code) rate is largely stable across agents ($9.7\%$--$13.9\%$), indicating that the gap with the human baseline is a shared trait of the agent population rather than an artefact of Codex alone. The \emph{Imprecise} share varies more widely, from $23.8\%$ (Copilot) to $41.1\%$ (OpenAI Codex) and $36.9\%$ (Cursor), and must be kept in mind when comparing per-agent percentages.

\paragraph{Per-language and per-task-type breakdowns.} Language-level patterns amplify the global picture: \emph{C} ($V_1$ $67.2\%$) and \emph{TypeScript} ($V_1$ $59.0\%$) are the two largest strata and contribute most of the global $V_1$ bias, whereas \emph{C\#} and \emph{Java} show the opposite profile ($V_2$ stronger than $V_1$, and more mixing/NC). Python is balanced ($V_1$ $44.5\%$, $V_2$ $28.0\%$). Per-task-type stratification is limited to AIDev-pop repositories but still instructive: \texttt{fix} PRs stand out by their reliance on \emph{CB} combinations ($18.6\%$, well above the global $9.0\%$), suggesting that bug fixes more often require interleaving the agent's change with base-branch evolution; \texttt{perf} and \texttt{refactor} PRs invert the typical $V_1$/$V_2$ ordering toward $V_2$, consistent with these tasks deliberately keeping the upstream version on top of the agent's draft.

\paragraph{Comparison with Ghiotto et~al.} Table~\ref{tab:ghiotto-compare} reports the strategy proportions from this study alongside those of \citet{ghiotto2020nature} and the absolute difference. Only CB ($0.0$~pp) falls within our 95\% Wilson CI; all other strategies lie outside the CI, indicating detectable differences. Substantively, the gaps are small for V1 ($-0.9$~pp), V2 ($-0.4$~pp), and CB ($0.0$~pp), moderate for CC ($+3.2$~pp) and NC ($-2.4$~pp). These differences are consistent with agents relying on structurally simple strategies more often than the human Java baseline, but the overall profiles are broadly similar.

\paragraph{Statistical tests.} A chi-squared test of independence between strategy and agent on the $75{,}752$ classifiable chunks yields $\chi^2(20){=}2186$, $p{<}0.001$, Cram\'{e}r's~$V{=}0.08$ (weak effect). Per-agent pairwise comparisons (Holm-corrected) show that the largest inter-agent differences are between Copilot and Devin ($V{=}0.28$, moderate), driven primarily by the $V_1/V_2$ polarity inversion ($+26.5$~pp in V1 for Copilot). The Imprecise rate also differs across agents ($\chi^2(4){=}2219$, $p{<}0.001$, $V{=}0.14$, moderate), ranging from $23.8\%$ (Copilot) to $41.1\%$ (OpenAI Codex). Clustering of chunks within the same merge commit means these~$p$-values are anti-conservative; Cram\'{e}r's~$V$ is the primary evidence of magnitude and should be treated as an upper bound.

\paragraph{Postponed resolutions.} Within the Imprecise bucket, a qualitatively distinct sub-category deserves separate attention: the $7{,}291$ \emph{Postponed} chunks ($6.0\%$ of all chunks; $15.9\%$ of all Imprecise). For these, the localization algorithm did find a resolution region, but the committed file still contained conflict markers (\verb|<<<<<<<|, \verb|=======|, \verb|>>>>>>>|)---meaning the merge was committed without actually resolving the conflict, leaving syntactically broken code in the repository. The Postponed rate is strikingly asymmetric across resolver types: agent resolvers produced only $69$ Postponed chunks out of $10{,}782$ agent-resolved chunks ($0.64\%$), compared to $7{,}190$ out of $109{,}575$ human-resolved chunks ($6.56\%$)---a roughly ten-fold difference. Among agents, the per-agent Postponed rate ranges from $0.58\%$ (Devin) to $3.30\%$ (Claude Code), all well below the human rate. The implications of this gap are discussed in Section~\ref{sec:disc-tools}.

\paragraph{Resolver-conditional distribution.} The strategy distribution differs markedly depending on whether the conflict was resolved by the agent or by a human; we present this analysis in full in Section~\ref{sec:rq3}, where it is contextualized alongside the resolver attribution (Figure~\ref{fig:rq3-resolver-strategy}).

\subsection{RQ3: Resolver}
\label{sec:rq3}

\paragraph{Overall resolver distribution.} Across the $50{,}700$ physical internal merges in our dataset, $47{,}812$ ($94.3\%$) are committed by a \emph{human}, $2{,}748$ ($5.4\%$) are committed by the \emph{agent} itself, and $140$ ($0.28\%$) are \emph{agent-assisted}, i.e., merge commits in which an agent-authored trailer (e.g., \texttt{Co-authored-by: devin-ai-integration[bot]}) appears alongside a human committer. The picture is therefore strongly \emph{human-led at the integration step}: even though agents open the PR and produce the feature branch, humans perform the merge in more than seventeen out of eighteen cases.

\paragraph{Per-agent contingency.} Table~\ref{tab:resolver-by-agent} cross-tabulates the PR author against the resolver of the internal merge. The agent-performed share differs by more than an order of magnitude across agents. Devin resolves its own merges most frequently ($24.3\%$, $575$ of $2{,}369$), followed by Cursor ($7.4\%$, $75$ of $1{,}013$), OpenAI Codex ($6.3\%$, $1{,}831$ of $29{,}250$), Claude Code ($6.3\%$, $54$ of $852$), and Copilot ($1.2\%$, $213$ of $17{,}216$). The \emph{agent-assisted} bucket is non-negligible only for Claude Code, where $7.6\%$ of merges carry a human committer plus an agent trailer, suggesting that Claude Code is more often used in an explicitly collaborative ``pair-programming'' mode than the other agents. In absolute terms, OpenAI Codex contributes most of the agent-resolved volume ($1{,}831$ merges, $66.6\%$ of all agent resolutions in the dataset), simply because it dominates the PR population.

\paragraph{Statistical tests.} A chi-squared test of independence between the resolver type (agent / human / agent-assisted) and the coding agent at the merge level yields $\chi^2(8){=}4003$, $p{<}0.001$, Cram\'{e}r's~$V{=}0.20$ (moderate effect), confirming that resolver propensity is not uniform across agents. Per-agent Wilson CIs on the self-resolution rate clearly separate Devin ($24.3\%$, 95\%~CI $[22.6\%,26.0\%]$) from Cursor ($7.4\%$, $[5.9\%,9.2\%]$), Claude Code ($6.3\%$, $[4.9\%,8.2\%]$), Codex ($6.3\%$, $[6.0\%,6.5\%]$), and Copilot ($1.2\%$, $[1.1\%,1.4\%]$). The agent-assisted bucket is non-negligible only for Claude Code ($7.6\%$, $[6.0\%,9.6\%]$); for all other agents the assisted CI is below $0.3\%$.

\paragraph{Resolver $\times$ strategy.} The resolver attribution recovers the single largest effect in our data (Figure~\ref{fig:rq3-resolver-strategy}). The \emph{Imprecise} share alone differs markedly: $16.7\%$ of chunks in agent-resolved merges fail to localize, versus $40.0\%$ of chunks in human-resolved merges, consistent with agents producing tighter, more clearly anchored resolutions. Conditioning on classifiable chunks, agent resolvers concentrate $79.7\%$ (95\%~CI $[78.8\%,80.5\%]$) of their decisions on $V_1$ and only $3.9\%$ on new code, whereas human resolvers spread their decisions across all strategies ($V_1$ $45.2\%$, $[44.8\%,45.5\%]$; $V_2$ $26.0\%$; $NC$ $11.5\%$; $CB$ $9.9\%$; $CC$ $6.9\%$). A chi-squared test of independence between strategy and resolver yields $\chi^2(5){=}3841$, $p{<}0.001$, Cram\'{e}r's~$V{=}0.23$ (moderate effect); this is the largest effect observed in the study. A sensitivity analysis treating all Imprecise chunks as V1 (upper bound) or as NC (lower bound) shows that the agent-to-human V1 ratio remains at $1.24{\times}$--$2.45{\times}$ across all scenarios, confirming that the finding is robust to the differential Imprecise rate. The $140$-merge agent-assisted sample is too small to support strong claims, but its distribution sits between the two regimes.

\textbf{Summary.} RQ3 shows that integration of agent-authored branches is still \emph{predominantly human-driven} ($94.3\%$ of internal merges are human-committed) and that the propensity of an agent to close its own merges varies by an order of magnitude across vendors, from rarely (Copilot, $1.2\%$) to roughly one merge in four (Devin, $24.3\%$). When agents do resolve, their strategy profile is strikingly different from both the PR-wide distribution and from the human baseline: $79.7\%$ $V_1$ selection, a near-absence of new-code resolutions, and a $2.4{\times}$-lower Imprecise rate than humans.

\begin{findingbox}
\textbf{Finding 6.} Integration of agent-authored branches is overwhelmingly human-led: $94.3\%$ of internal merges are committed by a human. The agent self-resolution rate varies by more than $20\times$ across vendors, from Copilot ($1.2\%$) to Devin ($24.3\%$). \\[3pt]
\textbf{Finding 7.} When agents resolve their own merges, they select $V_1$ in $79.7\%$ of classifiable chunks, far exceeding the human rate of $45.2\%$, and produce almost no new-code resolutions ($3.9\%$ vs.\ $11.5\%$). This is the largest single effect in the study (Cram\'{e}r's~$V{=}0.23$), and it suggests that agents fall back to the simplest possible resolution strategy when acting autonomously (Figure~\ref{fig:rq3-resolver-strategy}).
\end{findingbox}

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Key Findings and Implications}
\label{sec:disc-findings}

Our results reveal a coherent picture of \emph{conservative, human-delegated} merge conflict resolution in agent-authored branches. Three themes stand out.

\paragraph{Agents rely on verbatim strategies.} The global rate of verbatim side selection ($73.7\%$) matches the human Java baseline closely, but the within-verbatim structure differs: agents disproportionately favour the \emph{first parent} ($V_1$, $49.1\%$ vs.\ $V_2$, $24.6\%$). This asymmetry likely reflects the typical branch topology: the first parent ($p_1$) is the feature-branch tip authored by the agent, so taking $V_1$ amounts to keeping the agent's own code over the upstream changes. The exceptions (Claude Code and Devin systematically favour $V_2$) hint at agent-specific integration conventions: both tools may be configured to prefer pulling in the upstream state when conflicts arise. The reduced NC rate ($10.6\%$ vs.\ $13\%$) suggests that agents rarely synthesize a wholly new resolution when the two sides conflict, preferring instead to commit deterministically to one side. This conservatism makes agent resolutions more predictable but potentially less semantically correct than human resolutions that write new code.

\paragraph{Conflict incidence is agent- and language-specific.} The more than four-fold variation in merge-level conflict rates (Copilot $10.5\%$ to Cursor $47.8\%$) cannot be explained by code quality alone; it more likely reflects differences in branching strategy, PR lifetime, and rebasing practice. Copilot tends to produce short-lived, task-focused PRs with infrequent internal merges, while Cursor and Codex branches appear to live longer and incorporate more upstream changes. Language is also a moderating factor: C and TypeScript branches contribute the bulk of the $V_1$ bias, while C\# and C++ show more balanced or $V_2$-dominant profiles. Tooling designers and repository maintainers should therefore calibrate conflict-related settings per agent, not globally.

\paragraph{Human integration dominates.} The $94.3\%$ human resolver share indicates that, in the current ecosystem, agents open branches but rarely close their own merges. This division of labour exposes a gap in the agentic workflow: agents produce and propose changes, but the critical integration step (resolving conflicting regions and verifying semantic correctness) almost always falls to a human. The agent-resolved cases ($5.4\%$) are instructive precisely because they show what agents do when left to their own devices: they pick $V_1$ in $79.7\%$ of chunks, far exceeding the human baseline of $45.2\%$. This strong $V_1$ preference is consistent with a strategy of ``take my own code'' and move on, which minimises the risk of introducing bugs from the upstream version but also ignores the possibility that the upstream edit was intentional and important.

\subsection{Implications for Tool Design}
\label{sec:disc-tools}

Our findings have direct implications for the design of AI-assisted merge tools and CI/CD pipelines involving coding agents.

\paragraph{Targeted resolver augmentation.} Because human resolvers are responsible for $94.3\%$ of all resolutions, and they produce a richer strategy mix (including $11.5\%$ NC and $9.9\%$ CB), merge-assistance tools should be designed primarily for human reviewers, not agents. The human-directed resolution is more semantically nuanced and therefore more error-prone; tools that suggest reference resolutions or flag semantically suspicious verbatim picks would add the most value here. Conversely, agent-performed resolutions are highly predictable ($79.7\%$ $V_1$) and therefore easier to validate automatically: a CI check that flags agent-resolved merges where $V_1$ was not the unique minimum-diff choice would catch the cases most likely to contain silent regressions.

\paragraph{Conflict-complexity triage.} The median conflicting merge contains only two chunks of 2--3 LOC each, placing it well within the range that automated resolvers~\cite{dinella2022deepmerge,svyatkovskiy2022mergebert,dong2023mergegen} handle reliably. However, the heavy right tail (a small fraction of merges above the 99th percentile contains hundreds or thousands of chunks) indicates that a tiered escalation strategy is warranted: resolve the bulk of the conflict load automatically, then route the extreme tail to human reviewers or specialized tooling. The per-agent conflict rates suggest that this triage should be tuned per agent, since Copilot branches are far less likely to produce multi-chunk conflicts than Cursor or Codex branches.

\paragraph{Imprecise rate as a pipeline health signal.} The $37.7\%$ global Imprecise rate, and its $2.4\times$ variation across resolver types ($16.7\%$ for agent-resolved vs.\ $40.0\%$ for human-resolved), suggests that the Imprecise rate can serve as a \emph{health signal} in merge pipelines. A rising Imprecise rate in a project's history may indicate increasing structural complexity or expanding scope of merge-time edits, either of which warrants attention.

\paragraph{Agents rarely commit unresolved conflicts.}\label{sec:disc-postponed} Among the $7{,}291$ \emph{Postponed} chunks (Section~\ref{sec:rq2}), the committed file still contained conflict markers, meaning the codebase was left in a syntactically broken state at those locations. The resolver-type breakdown reveals a striking asymmetry: agent resolvers produced Postponed chunks at a rate of $0.64\%$ ($69$ of $10{,}782$ chunks), compared to $6.56\%$ for human resolvers ($7{,}190$ of $109{,}575$ chunks)---a roughly ten-fold difference. This gap most likely reflects structural differences in how agents and humans operate: coding agents typically execute within scripted, sandboxed workflows that can programmatically verify the absence of conflict markers before committing, whereas humans may commit under time pressure or after failed manual resolution attempts, inadvertently leaving markers in place. From a practical standpoint, Postponed commits are the easiest category of conflict resolution failure to detect automatically---a single grep for conflict markers in committed files suffices---yet the $6.56\%$ human rate suggests this check is far from universal in CI pipelines for AI-authored PRs. Repository maintainers who accept agent-authored PRs would benefit from adding this check as a mandatory gate.

\subsection{Collaborative Dynamics Between AI and Humans}
\label{sec:disc-collaboration}

The clearest take-away about human--AI collaboration from our data is that it is \emph{currently asymmetric}: agents author the branch, but humans integrate it in $94.3\%$ of cases. This mirrors earlier observations about bot-authored PRs~\cite{alfadel2023dependabot}, where human review is the norm even for routine, automated changes. The agent-assisted category ($0.28\%$) is too small to draw strong conclusions, but its presence suggests occasional hybrid workflows where agents actively assist human resolvers.

A notable exception is Devin ($24.3\%$ self-resolution rate), which is designed for more extended agentic workflows where the agent is expected to handle not just code generation but also branch maintenance. Claude Code occupies an intermediate position: its $7.6\%$ agent-assisted rate, significantly higher than other agents, suggests it is frequently used in a \emph{pair-programming} mode, where a human co-author watches and guides the agent through the merge. Future work should investigate whether these collaboration patterns correlate with PR acceptance rates and post-merge defect rates.

\section{Threats to Validity}
\label{sec:threats}

We organize threats following \citet{wohlin2012experimentation}.

\textbf{Construct validity.} Three threats arise from the way we operationalize ``merge conflict'' and ``resolution.'' Our pipeline replays three-way merges using \texttt{git merge-file} with the default \texttt{diff3} algorithm, so projects that configure custom merge drivers may yield different conflict counts than our replay; this limitation is shared with prior large-scale studies~\cite{ghiotto2020nature,boll2024semi,owhadikareshk2019} and is an accepted trade-off for reproducibility and scalability. When a merge commit introduces edits outside conflict boundaries (e.g., reformatting), the \textsc{LocalizeResRegion} anchor may fail and the chunk is classified as \emph{Imprecise}; we report Imprecise rates explicitly throughout, and the strategy classifier compares whitespace-normalized resolution text against both conflict sides, following \citet{ghiotto2020nature}, a normalization that is standard in the literature~\cite{ghiotto2020nature,elias2023mestre,camposjunior2022sbes} and makes our results directly comparable. Finally, our unit of analysis is the internal merge commit, which does not capture resolutions performed via interactive rebase (single-parent commits) or branch abandonment, so our study provides a lower-bound characterization, a limitation also shared by \citet{ghiotto2020nature}.

\textbf{Internal validity.} Two threats concern the attribution of merges to agents and the temporal snapshot of our data. We attribute each merge commit to an agent or a human based on the committer identity in the Git metadata, with the account-to-agent mapping derived from the AIDev dataset~\cite{liaidev2026} and aggregating all known bot signatures per agent; two minor sources of noise remain, as accounts may change login over time and a human could theoretically use a bot account, but stratified results always report per-stratum sample sizes so readers can assess the impact of any residual noise. In addition, the AIDev dataset was collected with a cutoff of August 1, 2025~\cite{liaidev2026}, so PRs that were still open at that date may have been subsequently merged, closed, or updated. The reader should bear in mind the snapshot nature of the data.

\textbf{External validity.} Several threats limit the generalizability of our findings. Our study is scoped to the full AIDev catalogue, restricted to public GitHub repositories, so our findings should not be directly extrapolated to low-visibility or private repositories, which may exhibit different conflict patterns due to differences in project maturity, team size, coding standards, and review practices; per-task-type analyses rely on the AIDev-pop tier (repositories with more than 100 stars), and whenever that stratification is shown, the restricted support is flagged explicitly. The distribution of agents in AIDev is heavily skewed toward Codex (approximately 76\% of in-scope PRs in this study; Table~\ref{tab:scope-per-agent}), and per-agent comparisons involving less represented agents (Claude Code: $852$ internal merges; Cursor: $1{,}013$) have wider confidence intervals and lower statistical power, which we address by reporting Wilson CIs for all per-agent proportions, making the precision of each estimate explicit; Cursor and Claude Code CIs, while wider, remain non-overlapping with the Copilot CI for the key metrics, supporting the qualitative conclusions. The five agents covered in this study represent the most prominent autonomous coding agents available as of mid-2025, but the landscape of AI coding agents evolves rapidly, and new agents with different architectures, prompting strategies, or integration workflows may exhibit different conflict resolution behaviors; our results establish the first empirical baseline for the current generation of agents, but they should be revisited as the ecosystem matures. Some repositories referenced in the AIDev dataset may have been deleted, made private, or become otherwise inaccessible between the time of data collection and the time of our cloning, and our pipeline covers $57{,}582$ repositories, compared to approximately $116{,}000$ in the full AIDev catalogue (this gap is primarily explained by repositories with no internal merge commits, i.e., fast-forward or squash-merge workflows); only $108$ repositories ($0.2\%$ of in-scope repos) were entirely lost to cloning failures. An additional $236{,}449$ merge SHAs across $88{,}339$ PRs ($27.4\%$ of in-scope repos) could not be recovered: these commits existed exclusively on \texttt{refs/pull/*/head} references that GitHub no longer exposes, as confirmed by a targeted recovery pass that fetched those refs for every affected repository and found $95.7\%$ of the SHAs permanently inaccessible. We do not expect this to introduce directional bias, since the choice of PR integration strategy is a repository policy largely orthogonal to how conflicts are resolved within the PR. Finally, $1{,}175$ merge SHAs across $626$ PRs in $31$ repositories exceeded the per-SHA timeout budget of the \textsc{LocalizeResRegion} algorithm; the resulting chunks are classified as \emph{Imprecise} and, since the total Imprecise count is $45{,}847$, timeout-driven failures account for a small fraction of all Imprecise chunks and have negligible influence on the reported strategy distributions.

\textbf{Conclusion validity.} Two threats affect our statistical conclusions. Conflict chunks within the same merge commit and across commits within the same repository are not fully independent, and this within-cluster correlation makes all $p$-values anti-conservative; as described in Section~\ref{sec:meth-analyses}, we address this by reporting Cram\'{e}r's~$V$ (bias-corrected~\cite{bergsma2013bias}) and Cliff's~$\delta$ as primary evidence of magnitude, treating both as upper bounds, and not using $p{<}0.05$ as evidence of practical importance, since at $n{\approx}60{,}000$ chunks statistical power is extreme (even a $0.1$~pp difference yields $p{<}0.001$), so substantive conclusions rest on absolute percentage-point differences and effect sizes rather than significance thresholds. As noted in Section~\ref{sec:meth-resolution}, agent-resolved chunks also have a substantially lower Imprecise rate ($16.7\%$) than human-resolved chunks ($40.0\%$), and this asymmetry may inflate the apparent~$V_1$ dominance among agent resolvers if $V_1$ resolutions are inherently easier to localize; the sensitivity analysis in Section~\ref{sec:rq3} bounds the agent-to-human $V_1$ ratio at $1.24{\times}$--$2.45{\times}$ across the full range of Imprecise reassignment scenarios, confirming that the qualitative conclusion holds even under the most conservative assumptions.

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented the first large-scale empirical study of merge conflicts in AI-authored pull requests, covering $50{,}700$ internal merge commits across $57{,}582$ repositories produced by five state-of-the-art coding agents. Our analysis yields three main conclusions.

\textbf{Structural nature (RQ1).} Merge conflicts in agent-authored branches are structurally small, with a median of two chunks of 2--3 LOC, but they exhibit a heavy right tail that concentrates most of the conflict workload in a small fraction of merges. Conflict incidence varies by more than $4{\times}$ across agents (Copilot $10.5\%$ to Cursor $47.8\%$), suggesting that branching strategy and PR lifetime, rather than code quality, are the primary drivers of conflict frequency.

\textbf{Resolution strategies (RQ2).} Among classifiable chunks, $73.7\%$ adopt one side verbatim, essentially matching the $75\%$ reported by \citet{ghiotto2020nature} for human Java merges, while agents introduce new code less often ($10.6\%$ vs.\ $13\%$). The overall distribution is conservative and broadly similar to the human baseline, with the inter-agent variation explained primarily by the $V_1/V_2$ polarity (some agents prefer their own code; others defer to upstream). A distinct sub-category of the Imprecise bucket---\emph{Postponed} resolutions---reveals an additional quality signal: $7{,}291$ chunks ($6.0\%$) were committed with unresolved conflict markers still present in the file. Human resolvers produce these at a rate of $6.56\%$, roughly ten times the agent rate of $0.64\%$, suggesting that agents almost never commit broken conflict state, whereas humans do so at an appreciable rate.

\textbf{Resolver (RQ3).} Integration is overwhelmingly human-led: $94.3\%$ of merges are committed by a human. When agents do resolve autonomously, they concentrate $79.7\%$ of decisions on~$V_1$, far exceeding the human rate of $45.2\%$, and produce almost no new-code resolutions, an effect confirmed across all sensitivity scenarios (Cram\'{e}r's~$V{=}0.23$).

\paragraph{Future work.} Several directions extend this study naturally. First, a \emph{longitudinal analysis} tracking how conflict rates and resolution strategies evolve as agents mature would establish whether the current baseline is stable or a transient artefact of early adoption. Second, \emph{semantic correctness assessment}, linking each resolution to downstream test outcomes or reviewer comments, would quantify the practical cost of the agent's conservative $V_1$ preference. Third, extending the study to \emph{private repositories} and to newer agent families would test the generalizability of our findings beyond public GitHub. Finally, the patterns documented here can serve as ground-truth labels for training specialized \emph{agentic merge resolution} models, a use case not previously studied.
