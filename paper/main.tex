\begin{abstract}
AI-powered coding agents such as Claude Code, OpenAI Codex, GitHub Copilot, Cursor, and Devin are increasingly contributing to open-source software development through pull requests (PRs). When these AI-authored branches incorporate upstream changes via merge commits, textual merge conflicts can arise and must be resolved---either by the AI agent itself or by a human collaborator. Despite the growing adoption of agentic AI in software development, little is known about how merge conflicts are resolved in this context: which strategies are employed, who performs the resolution, and what factors influence the choice of strategy. In this paper, we present a large-scale empirical study of merge conflict resolution in AI-authored PRs, building upon the AgenticFlict dataset of 142,652 PRs opened by five AI agents across 59,412 repositories. By reproducing real merge commits found within PR branches using three-way \texttt{diff3} merging and comparing the conflicting regions against the actual resolved files, we classify each conflict chunk into one of eight resolution strategies (V1, V2, ConcatV1V2, ConcatV2V1, Combination, New~Code, None, Imprecise) and identify whether the resolver was an AI agent or a human developer. We discuss the implications of these findings for the design of AI-assisted merge tools and for understanding the collaborative dynamics between AI agents and human developers.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

% Qual é o contexto em que esse trabalho está inserido?
Concurrent work is essential to large-scale software development.
Once developers finish their independent work, changes must be integrated and made available to other developers, typically through a merge tool that aims to automate as much as possible of the task of combining parallel changes~\cite{mens2002}.
Despite decades of research on merge techniques, none of them can fully automate the process: when concurrent modifications affect overlapping regions, the tool produces a \emph{merge conflict} that a developer must inspect, understand, and resolve manually~\cite{ghiotto2020nature, brun2011}.
It has been reported that 10 to 20 percent of all merge attempts result in a conflict~\cite{ghiotto2020nature, kasi2013}, and that resolving them is a difficult, time-consuming, and error-prone task~\cite{vale2022challenges, nelson2019}.
Surveys with developers reveal that 56\% of them have deferred resolving a conflict due to its complexity, the large size of the conflicting code, or the large number of conflicting locations~\cite{nelson2019}, and that factors such as the number of conflicting chunks, lines of code, and code complexity significantly influence the time required for resolution~\cite{vale2022challenges}.
To date, the most comprehensive characterization of merge conflicts in the human development context was provided by Ghiotto et al.~\cite{ghiotto2020nature}, who examined over 960,000 merges across 2,731 open-source Java projects and established a multi-dimensional empirical portrait of conflicting chunks, covering the number of chunks per failed merge, chunk size in lines of code, the resolution strategies chosen by developers (V1, V2, Concatenation, Combination, New Code, and None), and the relationships between conflict characteristics and resolution decisions.
A key finding of that study is that 87\% of the conflict resolutions are composed exclusively of lines already present in the conflicting versions, without introducing any new code~\cite{ghiotto2020nature}. This result, independently confirmed by subsequent work~\cite{dinella2022deepmerge, boll2024derivability, campos2022combining}, has become a central assumption for virtually all automated resolution techniques proposed since.

% Qual é o problema que motiva a existência desse trabalho?
Software engineering is entering a new era shaped by autonomous coding agents, such as OpenAI Codex, Devin, GitHub Copilot, Cursor, and Claude Code, that go beyond code completion to autonomously create branches, implement features, and open pull requests (PRs) alongside human developers~\cite{li2025aidev}.
This shift is no longer speculative: the AIDev dataset documents over 456,000 agent-authored PRs across more than 61,000 repositories, with OpenAI Codex alone producing over 400,000 PRs in less than two months since its release~\cite{li2025aidev, li2026aidev_msr}.
When an agent-authored branch incorporates upstream changes (for instance via \texttt{git merge main}), textual merge conflicts may arise just as they do in human-driven development.
However, we do not know whether the nature of the conflicts generated by agent-authored PRs resembles the nature of the conflicts generated by humans.
This question is urgent for three reasons.
First, existing automated resolution techniques were designed on top of Ghiotto et al.'s human-centric characterization, and their validity for agent-generated conflicts is an untested assumption.
Second, every conflict that the agent does not resolve falls back to a human developer, increasing the coordination overhead of agent-driven development.
Third, the AIDev literature itself lists the treatment of merge conflicts by coding agents as an open research direction~\cite{li2025aidev}.

% Como a literatura tratou esse problema até então e por que a solução dada pela literatura não foi suficiente?
Prior work on merge conflict resolution has focused exclusively on human developers.
Ghiotto et al.~\cite{ghiotto2020nature} characterized the nature of human-authored conflicts, and their findings have served as the empirical substrate on top of which a rich body of subsequent research has been conceived.
On the resolution side, approaches range from structured and semi-structured merge techniques that exploit the syntactic structure of programs~\cite{cavalcanti2019impact} to data-driven methods such as DeepMerge~\cite{dinella2022deepmerge} and MergeBERT~\cite{svyatkovskiy2022mergebert}, which learn resolution patterns from historical merge data, and MergeGen~\cite{dong2023mergegen}, which reframes resolution as a generation task rather than a classification task.
More recent work has applied large language models to generate resolutions~\cite{shen2023chatmerge} and search-based software engineering to explore the combinatorial space of candidate solutions~\cite{campos2025evaluation}.
On the prediction side, tools such as MESTRE~\cite{elias2023mestre} and RPredictor~\cite{aldndni2023rpredictor} use machine learning to predict which resolution strategy a developer is likely to adopt, while Boll et al.~\cite{boll2024derivability} studied the derivability of resolutions across 131,154 conflicting merge commits from 10,000 projects and found that 87.9\% of individual chunks can be derived from a fixed set of language-agnostic patterns.
All of these efforts share a common trait: they were designed, trained, and evaluated exclusively on conflicts produced by human developers.
On the agent side, the AIDev dataset~\cite{li2025aidev, li2026aidev_msr} cataloged hundreds of thousands of agent-authored PRs with rich metadata on authorship, review timelines, and task types, but does not provide conflict-level analysis.
More recently, Ogenrwot and Businge~\cite{ogenrwot2026agenticflict} introduced \textsc{AgenticFlict}, a large-scale dataset of 142,652 agent PRs processed through deterministic merge simulation, identifying approximately 29,000 PRs with conflicts and extracting over 336,000 fine-grained conflict regions.
While \textsc{AgenticFlict} represents an important step toward understanding integration friction in AI-assisted development, the dataset focuses on conflict \emph{detection} (recording conflict markers and line spans) rather than on characterizing the \emph{resolution}: which strategies are employed, what the structural properties of conflicts and resolutions are, and who performs the resolution.
To the best of our knowledge, no study has yet characterized the nature of merge conflicts in the agent-authored regime along the dimensions established by Ghiotto et al.

% Como o seu trabalho trata o problema?
In this paper, we replicate and adapt Ghiotto et al.'s analytical framework over AIDev-pop, the curated subset of AIDev restricted to repositories with more than 100 stars, comprising approximately 33,600 PRs across 2,807 repositories~\cite{li2026aidev_msr}.
Unlike \textsc{AgenticFlict}, which simulates the merge between the PR head and base branches, our study targets the \emph{internal merge commits} within PR branches, that is, merge commits created when the agent (or a human collaborator) pulls upstream changes into the feature branch.
For each such merge, we replay the three-way merge between its parents, extract every conflicting chunk, localize the resolution actually adopted in the merge commit, and classify it into one of the canonical strategies (V1, V2, Concatenation, Combination, New Code, None).
We further identify whether the conflict was resolved by the coding agent or by a human developer.
Specifically, we address the following research questions:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{RQ1 (Structural nature).} What are the distributions of the number of conflicting chunks per failed merge, the size of each version (\textit{v1}, \textit{v2}), and the size of the resolution?
    \item \textbf{RQ2 (Resolution strategies).} How are resolution decisions distributed across conflicting chunks, and how does this distribution compare to the one reported by Ghiotto et al.\ for human-authored merges?
    \item \textbf{RQ3 (Resolver).} Who resolves these conflicts: the coding agent itself or a human collaborator?
\end{itemize}

For every research question in which it is meaningful, results are stratified by coding agent, programming language, and PR task type.

\subsection{Identifying Merge Commits Within PR Branches}
\label{sec:meth-merges}

For each PR, we identify merge commits (commits with exactly two parents) that exist within the PR branch but not in the base branch:

\begin{verbatim}
git rev-list --merges base_oid..head_oid
\end{verbatim}

These are merges that the PR author (or collaborators) created to bring upstream changes into the feature branch---typically via \texttt{git merge main}. We exclude octopus merges (more than two parents) and do not consider the integration merge created by GitHub when the PR is accepted.

\subsection{Reproducing Merges and Extracting Conflict Chunks}
\label{sec:meth-conflicts}

For each merge commit with parents $p_1$ and $p_2$, we compute the merge base ($b = \text{merge-base}(p_1, p_2)$) and identify files modified on both sides using \texttt{git merge-tree}. For each such file, we reproduce the three-way merge using:

\begin{verbatim}
git merge-file -p --diff3 p1_file base_file p2_file
\end{verbatim}

We parse the resulting diff3 output to extract individual conflict chunks, each delimited by \texttt{<<<<<<<}, \texttt{|||||||}, \texttt{=======}, and \texttt{>>>>>>>} markers. Each chunk produces three text regions: \textit{v1} (from $p_1$), \textit{base} (from $b$), and \textit{v2} (from $p_2$).

\subsection{Localizing the Resolution}
\label{sec:meth-resolution}

For each conflict chunk, we retrieve the resolved file from the merge commit itself via \texttt{git show \{merge\_sha\}:\{file\_path\}}. Since this file contains the entire contents (including non-conflicting regions and other resolutions), we isolate the specific resolution text for each chunk using contextual anchoring: we identify the unique prefix and suffix surrounding the conflict region in the merged output and use them to extract the corresponding slice from the resolved file.

\subsection{Classifying Resolution Strategies}
\label{sec:meth-strategies}

We classify each resolved chunk into one of eight resolution strategies using the \texttt{identify\_resolution} function, which compares the normalized resolution text against \textit{v1} and \textit{v2}:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{V1} / \textbf{V2}: The resolution is identical to one side of the conflict.
    \item \textbf{ConcatV1V2} / \textbf{ConcatV2V1}: The resolution is the concatenation of both sides, in the given order.
    \item \textbf{Combination}: The resolution contains elements from both sides but is not a simple concatenation.
    \item \textbf{New Code}: The resolution introduces content not present in either side.
    \item \textbf{None}: Both sides and the resolution are empty.
    \item \textbf{Imprecise}: The resolution could not be reliably classified.
\end{itemize}

For the statistical analysis in RQ4, we also define an aggregated four-level taxonomy: \textit{take-one-side} (V1, V2), \textit{combine} (ConcatV1V2, ConcatV2V1, Combination), \textit{introduce-new} (New Code), and \textit{trivial-or-other} (None, Imprecise).

\subsection{Identifying the Resolver}
\label{sec:meth-resolver}

We determine who resolved each conflict by analyzing the author and committer metadata of the merge commit. We classify the resolver as \textit{AI}, \textit{Human}, or \textit{Unknown} based on a curated list of known AI agent signatures (e.g., \texttt{claude[bot]}, \texttt{openai-codex[bot]}, \texttt{copilot[bot]}, \texttt{cursor-agent[bot]}, \texttt{devin-ai-integration[bot]}).

\subsection{Textual Similarity Metrics}
\label{sec:meth-similarity}

For each resolved chunk, we compute the Gestalt pattern matching (longest common subsequence) similarity between the resolution and each side of the conflict (\textit{sim\_res\_v1}, \textit{sim\_res\_v2}), as well as between \textit{v1} and \textit{v2} themselves (\textit{sim\_v1\_v2}).
