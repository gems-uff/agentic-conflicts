\begin{abstract}
AI coding agents (Claude Code, OpenAI Codex, GitHub Copilot, Cursor, and Devin) now open pull requests (PRs) at scale; inside those branches, conflicts eventually arise when upstream changes are merged in. We present the first study of who resolves these conflicts and how each agent's choices differ from one another and from humans, across $14{,}960$ conflicting internal merge commits from AI-authored PRs. Humans commit $96.1\%$ of these conflicting merges, and the per-agent self-resolution rate varies more than $60{\times}$ across vendors. Within the $462$ agent-resolved conflicting merges, the five agents do not share a common resolution strategy: per-agent strategy distributions range from indistinguishable from humans (Claude Code) to strongly divergent (Codex), and pairwise inter-agent comparisons span from indistinguishable (Cursor vs.\ Devin) to nearly disjoint (Copilot vs.\ Codex). Data for two agents exhibit high repository concentration: $90.2\%$ of Codex's self-resolved conflicting merges come from one monorepo, and $83.3\%$ of Claude Code's from one ERP project; their profiles therefore reflect the dominant repository's workflow, not general patterns. Agents commit unresolved conflict markers ten times less often than humans ($0.64\%$ vs.\ $6.56\%$). Two implications follow: tooling for conflict resolution calibrated to a single agent will transfer poorly to the next, and empirical claims about AI-agent behaviour at this volume must surface single-repository concentration before they can be interpreted.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

The increasing adoption of AI-powered coding agents, such as Claude Code, OpenAI Codex, GitHub Copilot, Cursor, and Devin, represents a paradigm shift in software development workflows. These agents autonomously create branches, implement features, and open pull requests (PRs) in open-source repositories, contributing code alongside human developers at a scale that only became possible in the last two years. The AIDev catalogue~\cite{liaidev2026} alone documents over 930{,}000 AI-authored PRs across 116{,}000 repositories, positioning agentic contributions as a first-class citizen of modern collaborative development.

When an AI-authored branch needs to incorporate upstream changes (e.g., via \texttt{git merge main}), textual merge conflicts may arise. These conflicts must be resolved before the merged code can be committed, and the resolution may be performed by the AI agent itself, by a human developer, or collaboratively. Despite the scale of agentic contributions, two questions remain unanswered for this new population of merges: \emph{who} performs the integration step on AI-authored branches, and \emph{how} the agents that do close their own merges behave relative to each other and to humans.

Prior work on merge conflict resolution has focused exclusively on human developers~\cite{ghiotto2020nature,vale2022challenges,dinella2022deepmerge,svyatkovskiy2022mergebert,dong2023mergegen}; the one dataset that targets agentic PRs, AgenticFlict~\cite{agenticflict2025}, analyzes merges between the PR head and base branches, but does not distinguish AI from human actors in the integration step. In addition, they do not study the resolution strategies used for resolving these conflicts. Consequently, no prior study has separated AI-performed from human-performed resolutions on merge commits and characterized the strategies applied by each agent individually.

In this paper, we address these gaps by conducting an empirical study on internal merge commits within AI-authored pull requests from the AIDev catalogue~\cite{liaidev2026}. We identify and reproduce each internal merge, localize the developer's resolution for every conflicting chunk following \citet{dinella2022deepmerge}, classify each resolution using the \citet{ghiotto2020nature} taxonomy, and attribute each resolution to an AI agent or a human based on the committer identity~\cite{liaidev2025se3}. Our main contributions are: (i)~the first attribution of conflict resolutions to specific AI coding agents on real merge commits at scale; (ii)~a characterization of the resolution strategies used by AI agents and humans; (iii)~a replication package containing all collection and analysis scripts. Concretely, we answer the following research questions:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{RQ1: Who resolves the merge conflicts introduced in AI-authored PRs, and how does this vary across coding agents?} Whether conflicts are resolved by the agent autonomously or by a human has direct implications for pipeline design and trust in agentic workflows. We identify who commits each internal merge and how the propensity for self-resolution differs by agent.
    \item \textbf{RQ2: How do AI coding agents resolve their own merge conflicts, both individually and relative to one another?} Aggregating across agents can mask large vendor-specific differences. We compare each agent's per-agent resolution-strategy distribution against the human reference within our corpus, run pairwise agent-against-agent contrasts, stratify the heterogeneity by file category, and surface the repository-concentration that confounds two of the five agent profiles.
\end{itemize}

Our headline results, developed in Sections~\ref{sec:rq1}--\ref{sec:rq2}, are: among the $14{,}960$ conflicting internal merges, humans commit $96.1\%$ and the per-agent self-resolution rate spans more than~$60{\times}$ (Codex $0.5\%$ to Devin $29.4\%$); the five agents differ markedly from each other in resolution strategy (per-agent contrasts $V{\in}[0.02,0.32]$; pairwise inter-agent contrasts $V{\in}[0.08,0.90]$); and two of the five agents (Codex and Claude Code) are each dominated by a single repository ($>83\%$ of their self-resolved conflicting merges in each case), so their per-agent profile reflects the dominant repository's workflow, not the agent.

The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. Section~\ref{sec:background} presents background and related work. Section~\ref{sec:methodology} describes our methodology and pipeline. Section~\ref{sec:results} reports results for each RQ. Section~\ref{sec:discussion} discusses implications. Section~\ref{sec:threats} addresses threats to validity, and Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes.

\section{Background and Related Work}
\label{sec:background}

\subsection{Software Merging and Merge Conflicts}
\label{sec:bg-merging}

Collaborative development relies on branching, whereby developers work in parallel and later reconcile their contributions via \emph{merging}~\cite{mens2002survey}. Textual three-way merge, as implemented in tools such as \texttt{git merge}, automatically combines non-overlapping edits and reports a \emph{conflict} when changes overlap~\cite{mens2002survey,ghiotto2020nature,cavalcanti2019}. The \texttt{diff3} output additionally preserves the common ancestor between \verb+|||||||+ and \verb|=======|, providing a three-way view on which subsequent analyses can rely~\cite{dong2023mergegen,dinella2022deepmerge}.

Merge conflicts are frequent and time-consuming. \citet{ghiotto2020nature} inspected 960{,}366 merge scenarios across 2{,}731 Java projects and found that 11.9\% resulted in at least one conflict; comparable rates (10--20\%) have been reported in other ecosystems~\cite{vale2022challenges,lessenich2018indicators}. \citet{vale2022challenges} surveyed developers and identified four recurring challenges: understanding the conflict, deciding on a resolution, dealing with tooling, and coordinating with teammates. \citet{nelson2019lifecycle} complemented these findings with a life-cycle perspective, characterizing the understand--plan--resolve--verify workflow. Beyond line-based merging, semistructured approaches~\cite{apel2011semistructured,cavalcanti2019,accioly2018semistructured} reduce spurious conflicts, while proactive approaches~\cite{brun2011proactive,kasi2013cassandra} warn developers in advance. Finally, \citet{boll2024semi} showed that 87.9\% of conflicting chunks follow a small, language-agnostic catalogue, suggesting that semi-automation may be more feasible than previously assumed.

\subsection{Merge Conflict Resolution Strategies}
\label{sec:bg-strategies}

\paragraph{Taxonomy of resolutions.}
\citet{ghiotto2020nature} introduced the canonical taxonomy that classifies each resolved chunk by comparing the merged text against the two conflicting versions ($v_1$, $v_2$): the developer may adopt one version verbatim (\textit{V1}, \textit{V2}), concatenate both sides in either order (\textit{Concatenation}), interleave lines from both (\textit{Combination}), write content absent from both sides (\textit{New Code}), or resolve to an empty region (\textit{None}). %An additional \textit{Imprecise} label is used when the resolution cannot be reliably mapped to the conflict region (e.g., because the file was substantially reorganized).
Across the 175{,}805 Java chunks they studied, \textit{V1} accounts for 50\% of the resolutions, \textit{V2} for 25\%, \textit{New Code} for 13\%, \textit{Combination} for 9\%, and \textit{Concatenation} for 3\%. A very small percentage (0.07\%) of the resolutions were classified as \textit{None}. We adopt this taxonomy, which gives us six classifiable labels (V1, V2, CC, CB, NC, NN), directly comparable to the human baseline.

\paragraph{Automated resolution: classification and generation.}
ML-based resolvers have matured along two paradigms. The \emph{classification} paradigm (e.g., \textsc{DeepMerge}~\cite{dinella2022deepmerge} and \textsc{Merge\-BERT}~\cite{svyatkovskiy2022mergebert}) trains models to select a strategy label. The \emph{generation} paradigm (\textsc{MergeGen}~\cite{dong2023mergegen}, \textsc{ChatMerge}~\cite{shen2023chatmerge}) synthesizes resolution text directly, outperforming classifiers and capturing \textit{New Code} resolutions that label-pickers cannot emit. Search-based approaches (\textsc{SBCR}~\cite{camposjunior2024sbcr}, \citet{camposjunior2022sbes}) recommend resolutions using lines already present in the conflicting versions. Further specialized methods include template-based synthesis~\cite{pan2021synthesis}, and data-mining resolution strategy recommenders (e.g., \textsc{MESTRE}~\cite{elias2023mestre} and \textsc{RPredictor}~\cite{aldndni2023rpredictor}).

\paragraph{Prediction and benchmarks.}
Complementary to resolution, conflict \emph{prediction} aims to warn developers early. \citet{owhadikareshk2019} trained a lightweight classifier on 267{,}657 merge scenarios across seven languages and reported F1 above 0.80 on most projects, without running the full merge. \citet{brindescu2020planning} pushed the prediction target further, using chunk-level features (size, construct, number of edit locations) to estimate the \emph{time} a developer will spend on a conflict. \textsc{ConflictBench}~\cite{shenmeng2024conflictbench} curates 180 Java scenarios with ground-truth resolutions to standardize the evaluation of merge tools, addressing the reproducibility gap left by project-specific mining studies.

\paragraph{Gap.}
All the studies above treat \emph{humans} as the implicit resolvers. No prior work has separated AI-performed from human-performed conflict resolutions on AI-authored branches, characterized how individual coding agents differ in their resolution strategies, or examined how robust an aggregate agent-versus-human contrast is to the per-agent or per-repository composition of the corpus. Our work fills these gaps by reusing \citet{ghiotto2020nature} taxonomy on a population of merges that only became possible after the recent rise of autonomous coding agents and by reporting per-agent contrasts and explicit robustness checks alongside the aggregate.

\subsection{AI Agents in Software Development}
\label{sec:bg-agents}

The past two years have seen the emergence of autonomous coding agents that act as first-class contributors on GitHub. \citet{liaidev2025se3} label this regime \emph{Software Engineering 3.0}, in which agents open branches, write code, run tests, and submit pull requests reviewed by humans or other agents. \citet{peng2023copilot} reported a 55\% speed-up in a randomized controlled trial of GitHub Copilot, and \citet{wang2024llmagents} survey LLM-based agent architectures and benchmarks, prominently \textsc{SWE-bench}~\cite{jimenez2024swebench}. We target five representative agents: Anthropic's \textbf{Claude Code}, OpenAI's \textbf{Codex}, GitHub's \textbf{Copilot} (agentic mode), \textbf{Cursor}, and Cognition's \textbf{Devin}. Each authors commits under a distinctive bot identity (e.g., \texttt{claude[bot]}, \texttt{copilot[bot]}, \texttt{devin-ai-integration[bot]}), which makes attribution possible at scale.

Empirical studies of these agents remain scarce. \citet{liaidev2025se3,liaidev2026} characterize agentic PRs along dimensions such as acceptance rate, size, and review turnaround, but do not examine how branches are integrated. \citet{agenticflict2025} study simulated merges between the PR head and base commits to count conflicts, but do not examine the resolutions adopted by the developers. Earlier studies of bot-authored PRs~\cite{alfadel2021dependabot} established the methodology for studying bot contributions, but modern agents synthesize open-ended code rather than applying fixed templates, warranting dedicated study. We complement this prior work by analyzing merge commits within PR branches, distinguishing AI- from human-driven resolutions, and applying the human-centric taxonomy at the per-agent level rather than only on the aggregate.

\subsection{The AIDev and AgenticFlict Datasets}
\label{sec:bg-agenticflict}

Two datasets currently underpin empirical research on AI coding agents. \textbf{AIDev}~\cite{liaidev2026} catalogues 932{,}791 PRs opened by five agents (Claude Code, Codex, Copilot, Cursor, Devin) across 116{,}211 repositories and 72{,}631 developers, mined from the GitHub Archive. It provides the full PR metadata (title, body, diff summary, state, \texttt{base\_oid}, \texttt{head\_oid}, \texttt{merged\_at}) but no conflict information. \textbf{AgenticFlict}~\cite{agenticflict2025} adds a first conflict layer by locally cloning each repository and running a \texttt{git merge} between the PR's \texttt{base\_oid} and \texttt{head\_oid}, identifying 29{,}609 conflicting PRs (27.67\% rate) and 336{,}380 conflict regions. AgenticFlict shows that conflicts in agent-authored code are frequent and non-trivial, but it has two limitations that motivate our work: (i)~the conflicts are \emph{simulated} from the PR's tip commits and sometimes may not correspond to a real merge ever committed, so there are no \emph{resolutions} to study; and (ii)~no resolver actor exists in the simulated setup, so AI and human cannot be distinguished in integration. Our study addresses both gaps from AIDev directly: we look \emph{inside} each PR branch for real two-parent commits (``internal merges''), reproduce them with \texttt{git merge} and \texttt{diff3} output, localize the accepted resolution for every conflicting chunk, and attribute each resolution to the merge committer.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}

This study employs a multi-stage pipeline to systematically extract, reconstruct, and analyze merge conflicts from pull request histories in large-scale software repositories, as illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline}. Starting from the AIDev dataset, we identify in-scope pull requests, recover their commit histories, and isolate internal merge commits that incorporate upstream changes. These merges are then reproduced using three-way merge techniques to extract fine-grained conflict chunks, which serve as the unit of analysis. Each conflict is localized within the resolved code and classified into a standardized taxonomy of resolution strategies, while resolver attribution is inferred from commit metadata. The following sections describe each stage of this pipeline in detail, from data selection and merge identification to conflict reconstruction, resolution localization, and strategy classification

 \centering
 \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/pipelinev2}
 \caption{Overview of the data collection and analysis pipeline.}
 \label{fig:pipeline}
\end{figure}

\subsection{PR Selection and Scope}
\label{sec:meth-selection}

Our study is scoped to the full AIDev catalogue~\cite{liaidev2026} (approximately $930{,}000$ PRs from five agents across $116{,}000$ repositories). We consider PRs in all states (\textit{merged}, \textit{closed}, \textit{open}); restricting to merged PRs would bias toward histories rewritten by rebase or squash-merge and drop the intermediate merge commits we want to study. The per-PR commit data needed to locate internal merges is not shipped with AIDev, so we produce it ourselves by fetching \texttt{refs/pull/*/head} for every in-scope repository and enumerating, per PR, the commits between the fork-point with the base branch and the PR tip.

Applying this procedure yields a universe of $210{,}902$ in-scope PRs across $57{,}582$ repositories; the complete SHA list could not be found for $25{,}571$ of these PRs ($12.1\%$), mostly because of disconnected histories ($20{,}439$) or PR head references no longer reachable ($5{,}132$). %Table~\ref{tab:scope-per-agent} reports the per-agent distribution: OpenAI Codex dominates (approximately 76\% of the study's PRs), with Copilot, Cursor, Devin, and Claude Code covering the long tail.

\subsection{Identifying Merge Commits Within PR Branches}
\label{sec:meth-merges}

For each PR, we identify merge commits (commits with exactly two parents). These are merges created to bring upstream changes into the feature branch, distinct from the single ``integration merge'' that GitHub creates when a PR is accepted. We exclude octopus merges (three or more parents), following \citet{ghiotto2020nature}, and the GitHub integration merge itself: any two-parent commit whose subject matches \texttt{\textasciicircum{}Merge pull request \#$d$+} (case-insensitive) is filtered out, since GitHub's pre-merge already check them for conflicts before integration. This step excluded $70{,}042$ integration-merge SHAs and $93$ octopus-merge SHAs.

\paragraph{Merge-level deduplication.} A single merge commit can appear on the head branches of several PRs (force-push, re-open, cherry-pick), so naive per-PR indexing overcounts the same artefact. All merge- and chunk-level statistics use natural keys that discard the PR identifier: (repository, merge SHA) for merges and (repository, merge SHA, file path, chunk index) for chunks. Applying these filters returns $452{,}114$ raw PR-level merge references, which collapse to $50{,}700$ distinct merge commits ($8.92\times$ inflation); the most extreme single merge appears on $848$ distinct PRs. Only $12{,}299$ PRs ($5.8\%$ of the in-scope PRs) contribute at least one internal merge.

\subsection{Reproducing Merges and Extracting Conflict Chunks}
\label{sec:meth-conflicts}

For each merge commit with parents $p_1$ and $p_2$, we compute the merge base ($b = \text{merge-base}(p_1, p_2)$) and identify files modified on both sides using \texttt{git merge-tree}. For each such file, we reproduce the three-way merge using:

\begin{verbatim}
git merge-file -p --diff3 p1_file base_file p2_file
\end{verbatim}

We parse the resulting diff3 output to extract individual conflict chunks, each delimited by \verb|<<<<<<<|, \verb+|||||||+ , \verb|=======|, and \verb|>>>>>>>| markers. Each chunk produces three text regions: \textit{v1} (from $p_1$), \textit{base} (from $b$), and \textit{v2} (from $p_2$). For each chunk we also record the non-blank line count of each side, computed after trimming whitespace, consistent with \citet{ghiotto2020nature} normalization.

\subsection{Localizing the Resolution}
\label{sec:meth-resolution}

For each conflict chunk, we retrieve the resolved file from the merge commit and apply the \textsc{LocalizeResRegion} algorithm of \citet{dinella2022deepmerge}, which isolates the specific resolution text by anchoring on the unique textual context (prefix and suffix) surrounding the conflict region in the three-way merge output. If unique anchors cannot be found, the chunk is marked \emph{Imprecise}. \citet{dinella2022deepmerge} validated the algorithm on 174 conflict regions from 50 randomly selected merges and found 100\% correct localization (95\%~CI: $[0.979, 1.0]$); we use it on this basis. We also record the non-blank line count of the resolution text.

A potential confound is a \emph{differential Imprecise rate} across resolver types: if localization fails at different rates for agent- and human-resolved chunks, the observed strategy distributions may not be directly comparable. To bound this effect, we conduct a sensitivity analysis that reassigns all Imprecise chunks to $V_1$ (upper bound for $V_1$ dominance) or to NC (lower bound), and assess whether the per-agent contrasts survive across both extremes. The results of this analysis are reported in Section~\ref{sec:threats}.

\subsection{Classifying Resolution Strategies}
\label{sec:meth-strategies}

We classify each resolved chunk into one of six resolution strategies, following the taxonomy of \citet{ghiotto2020nature}, by comparing the normalized resolution text against both conflict sides:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{V1} / \textbf{V2}: The resolution is identical to one side of the conflict.
    \item \textbf{CC} (\emph{Concatenation}): The resolution is the concatenation of both sides in either order (we fold both orderings into \textit{CC} to stay comparable with \citet{ghiotto2020nature}).
    \item \textbf{CB} (\emph{Combination}): The resolution interleaves lines from both sides without introducing new lines.
    \item \textbf{NC} (\emph{New Code}): The resolution introduces content not present in either side.
    \item \textbf{NN} (\emph{None}): The resolution is empty.
    \item \textbf{Imprecise}: The resolution could not be reliably isolated (Section~\ref{sec:meth-resolution}) and is kept as a separate bucket to avoid silent misclassification.
\end{itemize}

Because \citet{ghiotto2020nature} do not report an \textit{Imprecise} bucket, RQ2 distributions are reported over the six classifiable labels, with the \textit{Imprecise} share stated separately.

\subsection{File-Path Categorisation}
\label{sec:meth-filecats}

To condition the strategy analysis on what kind of file each conflict occurs in, we assign each conflicting file to one of six mutually exclusive categories using a deterministic, priority-ordered heuristic applied to the repository-relative file path. The categories and their matching rules are as follows, applied in the order listed (the first matching rule wins):

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{lock} --- the file's basename exactly matches one of 16 known dependency lock-file names across all major ecosystems represented in the dataset: \texttt{package-lock.json}, \texttt{yarn.lock}, \texttt{pnpm-lock.yaml}, \texttt{npm-shrinkwrap.json}, \texttt{Pipfile.lock}, \texttt{poetry.lock}, \texttt{uv.lock}, \texttt{Gemfile.lock}, \texttt{composer.lock}, \texttt{Cargo.lock}, \texttt{go.sum}, \texttt{mix.lock}, \texttt{flake.lock}, \texttt{pubspec.lock}, \texttt{Podfile.lock}, and \texttt{bun.lockb}.
    \item \textbf{generated} --- the file path ends with one of the suffixes \texttt{.min.js}, \texttt{.min.css}, \texttt{.bundle.js}, \texttt{.map}, or \texttt{.snap}; or the path traverses a known build-output or dependency directory (\texttt{dist/}, \texttt{build/}, \texttt{out/}, \texttt{node\_modules/}, \texttt{vendor/}, \texttt{target/}, \texttt{coverage/}, \texttt{\_\_pycache\_\_/}, \texttt{.next/}, \texttt{.nuxt/}, \texttt{.cache/}, or \texttt{.parcel-cache/}).
    \item \textbf{doc} --- the file extension is one of \texttt{.md}, \texttt{.rst}, \texttt{.txt}, or \texttt{.adoc}.
    \item \textbf{config} --- the file extension is one of \texttt{.json}, \texttt{.yaml}, \texttt{.yml}, \texttt{.toml}, \texttt{.ini}, \texttt{.cfg}, \texttt{.conf}, or \texttt{.env}.
    \item \textbf{migration} --- the file extension is \texttt{.sql} or the path contains a \texttt{/migrations/} or \texttt{/migration/} segment.
    \item \textbf{source} --- all remaining files, treated as source code.
\end{itemize}

The heuristic is basename- and path-prefix-based and is ecosystem-agnostic by design; its known limitation, that some files (e.g., \texttt{package.json} in a monorepo) can be functionally generated artefacts yet fall into \emph{config} rather than \emph{generated}, is discussed in Section~\ref{sec:threats}.

\subsection{Identifying the Resolver}
\label{sec:meth-resolver}

We determine who resolved each conflict from the author metadata of the merge commit using a substring/suffix heuristic over the author login: a login that ends in \texttt{[bot]} or contains any of \texttt{copilot}, \texttt{devin}, \texttt{claude}, \texttt{cursor}, \texttt{openai} is labelled \textit{Agent}; otherwise, if a \texttt{Co-authored-by:} trailer in the commit message matches the same rule, the merge is labelled \textit{Agent-assisted}; otherwise it is \textit{Human}. The PR-authoring agent is recorded separately, taken from AIDev's bot-account mapping~\cite{liaidev2025se3,liaidev2026}. When per-agent strategy distributions are reported (Section~\ref{sec:rq2-heterogeneity} onward), the per-agent slice is defined as chunks where the resolver is \textit{Agent} and the PR-authoring agent is the same one named in the row; the proxy and the heuristic's known failure modes are discussed in Section~\ref{sec:threats}.

\subsection{Analyses per Research Question}
\label{sec:meth-analyses}

\textbf{RQ1: Resolver attribution.} We report the distribution of resolver type (agent-resolved, agent-assisted, human-resolved) at the merge-commit level, globally and per agent, with per-agent Wilson confidence intervals~\cite{wilson1927probable}.

\textbf{RQ2: Strategies and heterogeneity.} We report the relative frequency of each resolution label (V1, V2, CC, CB, NC, NN) at the chunk level for each agent and for the human-resolved subset, with the \textit{Imprecise} share reported separately. We then test the heterogeneity of the strategy distribution across agents by (i)~comparing each agent against the human-resolved subset of our own corpus in a $2{\times}$~strategy table, (ii)~running pairwise agent-against-agent contrasts with Bonferroni-corrected $p$-values across the ten agent pairs, and (iii)~stratifying each agent's strategy distribution by conflicting file category (\emph{source}, \emph{lock}, \emph{config}, \emph{generated}, \emph{doc}, \emph{migration}). We complement these contrasts with a per-agent repository-concentration analysis (top-1 repo share among self-resolved merges) and a focused case study of the two most concentrated agents.

\paragraph{Baseline calibration.} The strategy distribution of the $65{,}798$ chunks resolved by humans in our own corpus matches the Java baseline of \citet{ghiotto2020nature} to within five percentage points on every strategy ($V_1$: $45.1$ vs.\ $50.0$; $V_2$: $26.0$ vs.\ $25.0$; CC: $6.8$ vs.\ $3.0$; CB: $9.9$ vs.\ $9.0$; NC: $11.4$ vs.\ $13.0$; NN: $0.7$ vs.\ $0.0$). On this basis, the human-resolved subset of our corpus is used throughout the paper as the single human reference distribution; comparisons against \citet{ghiotto2020nature} are not revisited in the results.

\paragraph{Statistical analyses.}
All proportions are reported with 95\% Wilson confidence intervals~\cite{wilson1927probable}. For association between categorical variables (RQ1, RQ2) we use chi-squared tests with the bias-corrected Cram\'{e}r's~$V$~\cite{bergsma2013bias} as primary effect-size metric ($V{<}0.10$: weak; $0.10$--$0.30$: moderate; ${\geq}0.30$: strong). At $n{\approx}60{,}000$ chunks virtually any difference yields $p{<}0.001$, so magnitude carries more information than significance. Conflict chunks within the same merge and repository are not fully independent, so $p$-values are anti-conservative and all~$V$ values are treated as \emph{upper bounds}. For per-merge structural distributions (e.g., chunks per merge) we use Kruskal--Wallis with post-hoc Dunn tests (Bonferroni-corrected)~\cite{dunn1964multiple}; effect sizes are Cliff's~$\delta$~\cite{cliff1993dominance} since distributions are right-skewed.

\subsection{Dataset Overview}
\label{sec:res-overview}

After merge-level deduplication, our pipeline covers $50{,}700$ distinct internal merge commits authored inside the $12{,}299$ PRs across $4{,}806$ repositories (Table~\ref{tab:dataset-overview}). Of those merges, $14{,}960$ ($29.5\%$) produce at least one textual conflict, yielding $121{,}599$ conflict chunks. At the PR level, $12{,}299$ PRs ($5.8\%$ of all in-scope PRs) contribute at least one internal merge and $7{,}268$ ($3.4\%$) contain at least one conflicting merge, indicating that internal merges are the exception rather than the rule in agent-authored PRs and that conflicts, when they do arise, cluster inside a comparatively small slice of the dataset. The resolution-localization algorithm of \citet{dinella2022deepmerge} uniquely identifies the resolution region for $83{,}043$ of the $121{,}599$ chunks ($68.3\%$); the remaining $38{,}556$ ($31.7\%$) failed to find unique anchors and are kept as \emph{Imprecise}. Within the localized chunks, $7{,}291$ ($6.0\%$ of all chunks) are \emph{Postponed}: localization succeeded but the committed file still contained unresolved conflict markers (\verb|<<<<<<<|, \verb|=======|, \verb|>>>>>>>|), meaning the conflict was committed without being resolved. We fold Postponed into the analytical Imprecise bucket alongside anchor failures (combined $45{,}847$ chunks, $37.7\%$) to avoid silent misclassification; the Postponed sub-category is analyzed further in Section~\ref{sec:rq2-postponed}.

\subsection{RQ1: Who Resolves the Conflicts?}
\label{sec:rq1}

\paragraph{Overall resolver distribution.} Among the $14{,}960$ conflicting internal merges, $14{,}381$ ($96.1\%$) are merged by\emph{humans}, $462$ ($3.1\%$) by the \emph{agent}, and $117$ ($0.8\%$) are \emph{agent-assisted}, i.e., merges where an agent-authored trailer (e.g., \texttt{Co-authored-by: devin-ai-integration[bot]}) appears alongside a human committer. Even when an internal merge within an agent-authored PR \emph{produces a conflict}, requiring an explicit resolution,  the integration step remains overwhelmingly human-led: humans perform the merge in about 96\% of the cases.

\paragraph{Per-agent contingency.} Table~\ref{tab:resolver-by-agent} cross-tabulates the PR-authoring agent against the resolver of the internal merge conflicts. \subsection{RQ2: How Do Agents Resolve Their Own Merges?}
\label{sec:rq2}

We now turn to the strategy distribution within the $10{,}782$ chunks inside internal merges committed by agents, using the human-resolved subset of our corpus ($109{,}575$ chunks) as the reference. Considering only the chunks where the resolution was classified,  we have $8{,}979$ chunks resolved by agents, $975$ chunks resolved with human-agent collaboration and $65{,}798$ chunks resolved by humans. We present the per-agent and pairwise contrasts (Section~\ref{sec:rq2-heterogeneity}), show that two of the five agents are dominated by a single repository whose pattern shapes their entire profile (Section~\ref{sec:rq2-codex}), and close with the asymmetry in unresolved-marker commits between agents and humans (Section~\ref{sec:rq2-postponed}).

\paragraph{File-category conditioning.} Part of the per-agent heterogeneity is driven by file-type composition rather than resolution strategy alone. \subsubsection{Single-repository concentration as a confound}
\label{sec:rq2-codex}

The Codex outlier in Table~\ref{tab:per-agent-vs-human} is not, in fact, a generic ``Codex picks $V_1$'' behaviour. Of the $61$ Codex self-resolved merges, $55$ ($90.2\%$) come from a single repository, the \texttt{BlazingSu/nocobase} monorepo, and exhibit a near-identical profile: median $121$ chunks per merge, $99.9\%$ $V_1$, $\sim$$92\%$ one-line chunks. The dominant path within those merges is consistently \texttt{packages/core/app/package.json}: a single file edited in $\sim 121$ regions, each of which is a one-line dependency-version bump (e.g., \texttt{"package-X": "\textasciicircum{}1.2.3"} on one side, a different version on the other), resolved by taking the agent's side ($V_1$) verbatim. These $55$ merges have distinct SHAs that modify different version strings and so cannot be collapsed further by deduplication; they contribute $6{,}590$ of Codex's $6{,}734$ self-resolved chunks ($98\%$), and roughly $60$--$70\%$ of all agent-resolved chunks in the entire corpus.

The same pattern applies to Claude Code at smaller scale. Of the $12$ Claude Code self-resolved merges, $10$ ($83.3\%$) come from a single repository, \texttt{itdojp/ITDO\_ERP2}, and concentrate in three Python source files of an enterprise-resource-planning project (\texttt{test\_users\_extended.py}, \texttt{customer.py}, \texttt{budget.py}). Table~\ref{tab:repo-concentration} reports the top-1 and top-3 repository shares for each agent. Codex and Claude Code are the only two agents with top-1 share above $80\%$; the other three operate across $36$--$123$ distinct repositories with no single repository contributing more than $10\%$ of their merges. The interpretation is methodological: for agents with concentrated samples, the per-agent strategy distribution is a property of the dominant repository's workflow, not a population-level claim about the agent. The $V{=}0.02$ for Claude Code in Table~\ref{tab:per-agent-vs-human} reflects $12$ correlated merges, not $12$ independent observations of agent behaviour, and we abstain from interpreting it as ``Claude Code matches humans''.

\subsubsection{Postponed: Agents Rarely Commit Unresolved Conflicts}
\label{sec:rq2-postponed}

Within the Imprecise bucket, a qualitatively distinct sub-category deserves separate attention: the $7{,}291$ \emph{Postponed} chunks ($6.0\%$ of all chunks; $15.9\%$ of all Imprecise). For these, the localization algorithm did find a resolution region, but the committed file still contained conflict markers (\verb|<<<<<<<|, \verb|=======|, \verb|>>>>>>>|), meaning the merge was committed without actually resolving the conflict, leaving syntactically broken code in the repository. The Postponed rate is strikingly asymmetric across resolver types: agent resolvers produced only $69$ Postponed chunks out of $10{,}782$ agent-resolved chunks ($0.64\%$), compared to $7{,}190$ out of $109{,}575$ human-resolved chunks ($6.56\%$), a roughly ten-fold difference. Among agents, the per-agent Postponed rate ranges from $0.58\%$ (Devin) to $3.30\%$ (Claude Code), all well below the human rate.

\begin{findingbox}
\textbf{Finding 4.} Agent resolvers commit \emph{Postponed} chunks (files still containing conflict markers) at $0.64\%$, versus $6.56\%$ for humans, a $10{\times}$ difference. The $7{,}291$ Postponed chunks ($6.0\%$ of all chunks) are syntactically broken code committed without resolving the conflict, almost entirely by humans; a CI-level grep would catch all of them.
\end{findingbox}

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Heterogeneity, Not a Conservative Approach}
\label{sec:disc-findings}

Had we aggregated the five agents into a single ``agent'' bucket, the data would have produced a moderate ``agents are more conservative than humans'' contrast (Cram\'{e}r's $V{=}0.23$). The per-agent disaggregation (Sections~\ref{sec:rq2-heterogeneity}--\ref{sec:rq2-codex}) shows that this reading does not survive. Three of the five agents (Copilot, Cursor, Devin) sit at $V{\leq}0.09$ from the human reference and actually invert the $V_1$/$V_2$ polarity the aggregate suggests, leaning toward $V_2$ rather than $V_1$. The two extremes of the per-agent table both turn out to be repository artefacts: Codex's $V{=}0.32$ is concentrated in $55$ merges from a single \texttt{BlazingSu/nocobase} \texttt{package.json} pattern, and Claude Code's $V{=}0.02$ reflects $12$ merges concentrated in a single ERP repository. Pairwise contrasts confirm the picture from another angle: Cursor and Devin are statistically indistinguishable, while Codex is two to three Cram\'{e}r's-$V$ steps away from every other agent. The substantive story is therefore not that ``agents are conservative,'' but that the five agents analyzed behave very differently from each other; calibrating tooling against a single agent risks transferring poorly to the next.

\paragraph{Against treating AI as a single object.} A growing literature reports performance numbers for ``AI'' on software-engineering tasks as if the underlying systems formed a single class~\cite{liaidev2025se3,peng2023copilot,jimenez2024swebench,wang2024llmagents}, possibly without distinguishing which agent (or which underlying model) produced each output. Our results push back against that framing in the specific case of merge-conflict resolution: even after fixing the task, the same human reference, and the same time period, the five agents we observed produce strategy distributions whose pairwise distance covers nearly the full possible range of Cram\'{e}r's $V$ (from indistinguishable to nearly disjoint). We acknowledge that the distinction between \emph{agent} and \emph{underlying language model} is itself a confound that we cannot fully control: the same agent may be backed by different model versions over time, and we have no per-merge visibility into which model handled each commit. But that confound \emph{strengthens} rather than weakens the argument: if the observed heterogeneity is at least partly downstream of model choice, then aggregate ``AI vs.\ humans'' claims that paper over both the agent and the model layers will be even less stable than ours. For empirical AI-agent studies in software engineering, our recommendation is to report behaviour at least at the agent level, alongside the dominant repositories from which each agent's data is drawn (Section~\ref{sec:rq2-codex}); we believe this is a precondition for any external-validity claim about ``what AI does.''

\subsection{Implications for Tool Design}
\label{sec:disc-tools}

\paragraph{Per-agent calibration over global heuristics.} Because the per-agent resolution profile varies by more than an order of magnitude in Cram\'{e}r's~$V$ and is in part driven by what each agent worked on (Copilot and Devin processed mostly lock files, where $V_2$ is the conventional resolution; Codex on its dominant repository collapsed to $V_1$ on \texttt{package.json} version bumps), merge-assistance tools that train or threshold against the union of all five agents will be miscalibrated for any individual one. Vendors and pipeline operators should track per-agent strategy distributions, condition on file category, and apply per-agent guardrails rather than treating the agent population as a single entity.

\paragraph{Targeted resolver augmentation.} Because humans perform $96.1\%$ of conflict resolutions and produce a richer strategy mix ($11.4\%$ NC, $9.9\%$ CB), merge-assistance tools should be designed primarily for human reviewers; tools suggesting reference resolutions or flagging semantically suspicious verbatim picks add the most value. Conversely, the small agent-resolved share is highly predictable in some configurations (e.g., Codex on bulk monorepo updates), where a CI check that flags non-minimum-diff resolutions would catch silent regressions cheaply.

\paragraph{Conflict-complexity triage.} The median conflicting merge contains only two chunks of 2--3 LOC on each side, well within the range that automated resolvers~\cite{dinella2022deepmerge,svyatkovskiy2022mergebert,dong2023mergegen, camposjunior2024sbcr} handle reliably; the heavy right tail justifies tiered escalation, with the extreme tail routed to human reviewers. The per-agent rates (Table~\ref{tab:scope-per-agent}) suggest tuning this triage per agent.

\paragraph{Agents rarely commit unresolved conflicts.}\label{sec:disc-postponed} Among the $7{,}291$ Postponed chunks (Section~\ref{sec:rq2-postponed}), the committed file still contained conflict markers, leaving the code syntactically broken. The asymmetry is striking: agents produced Postponed chunks in $0.64\%$ of resolutions versus $6.56\%$ for humans, likely because agents typically execute within scripted workflows that programmatically verify absence of markers before committing, whereas humans may commit under time pressure. Postponed commits are also the easiest failure mode to catch automatically (a grep for conflict markers suffices), yet the $6.56\%$ human rate suggests this check is far from universal in CI pipelines for AI-authored PRs.

\subsection{Methodological Lesson and Collaboration Dynamics}
\label{sec:disc-methodology}

A secondary contribution of this paper is methodological. At first glance, the agent-resolved subset of $462$ conflicting merges and $10{,}782$ classifiable chunks looks adequate for a contingency analysis, but two of the five agents are dominated by a single repository: $90.2\%$ of Codex's self-resolved conflicting merges come from \texttt{BlazingSu/nocobase} and $83.3\%$ of Claude Code's from \texttt{itdojp/ITDO\_ERP2} (Table~\ref{tab:repo-concentration}). Without surfacing that concentration, the aggregate $V{=}0.23$ and the per-agent $V{=}0.02$ for Claude Code would both have read as population-level claims rather than as properties of single repositories. Two practices generalize beyond this study: (i)~report the most active repository per cell of any contingency table that involves AI-agent attribution, since agentic workflows can repeat the same scripted pattern thousands of times within a single project, and (ii)~stratify per-agent distributions by file category, since file-type composition (especially the share of lock files) drives a meaningful portion of the apparent strategy heterogeneity. Substantively, our data also indicates that human--AI collaboration is \emph{currently asymmetric}: agents author the branch, but humans resolve the conflicts inside it in $96.1\%$ of cases, mirroring earlier observations about bot-authored PRs~\cite{alfadel2021dependabot}. Devin ($29.4\%$ self-resolution on conflicting merges) and Claude Code ($24.1\%$ agent-assisted, plausibly a pair-programming mode) deviate sharply from this baseline; whether these collaboration patterns correlate with PR acceptance and post-merge defect rates is left to future work.

% ============================================================
\section{Threats to Validity}
\label{sec:threats}

We organize threats following \citet{wohlin2012experimentation}.

\textbf{Construct validity.} Our pipeline replays three-way merges using \texttt{git\ merge\-file} with the default \texttt{diff3} algorithm; projects with custom merge drivers may therefore yield different conflict counts, a limitation shared with prior large-scale studies~\cite{ghiotto2020nature,boll2024semi,owhadikareshk2019}. When a merge commit edits outside conflict boundaries, the \textsc{LocalizeResRegion} anchor may fail and the chunk is reported as \emph{Imprecise}; we report Imprecise rates explicitly. The classifier uses whitespace-normalized resolution text following \citet{ghiotto2020nature}, a standard normalization in the literature~\cite{elias2023mestre,camposjunior2022sbes}. Our unit of analysis is the internal merge commit, so we do not observe resolutions performed via interactive rebase or branch abandonment; our results should thus be interpreted as a lower bound on observed in-PR conflict resolution activity. The path-category labels used in Section~\ref{sec:rq2-codex} (lock, generated, config, doc, source, migration) are basename- and prefix-based heuristics that do not capture all build artefacts in every ecosystem; consequently, file-type composition effects may be misestimated. The \texttt{packages/core/app/package.json} pattern that drives the Codex outlier illustrates that \texttt{package.json} can act as configuration in some monorepos and as a generated artefact in others.

\textbf{Internal validity.} Two attribution caveats apply to the per-agent partition of Table~\ref{tab:per-agent-vs-human}. First, we use the PR-authoring agent (from AIDev) as a proxy for the merge-committing agent: this is exact under the canonical workflow where each agent commits only inside its own bot-authored PRs, but inexact if a developer invokes a different agent inside an already-open PR. Second, the substring/suffix heuristic for the resolver (Section~\ref{sec:meth-resolver}) introduces false positives (generic automation bots such as \texttt{kodiakhq[bot]} and \texttt{mergify[bot]}, and human logins containing matching substrings such as \texttt{devinkeehner}) and false negatives for agents committing under non-matching accounts. The dominant findings (Codex single-repo concentration, the order-of-magnitude per-agent $V$ spread, the $10{\times}$ Postponed asymmetry) are unlikely to be reversed under reasonable levels of such misclassification; however, smaller-$n$ rows of Table~\ref{tab:per-agent-vs-human} (Claude Code $n{=}108$, Cursor $n{=}165$, Copilot $n{=}776$) should be interpreted with this noise in mind, and we abstain from fine-grained ranking at sub-$V{=}0.10$ resolution.

\textbf{External validity.} Our scope is the public-GitHub portion of AIDev, so findings should not be extrapolated directly to private repositories. The agent distribution is heavily skewed toward Codex (approximately 76\% of in-scope PRs); within the agent-resolved subset, the Codex slice is itself dominated by one repository (\texttt{BlazingSu/nocobase}), and we make no claim that other repositories using Codex would reproduce the same $V_1$ collapse. The five agents are the most prominent as of mid-2025, and our baselines should be revisited as the ecosystem matures. Our pipeline reaches $57{,}582$ of the approximately $116{,}000$ AIDev repositories; the gap is primarily due to repositories or PR head references no longer accessible at fetch time (private, deleted, or with refs that GitHub no longer exposes). Within the reached repositories, $108$ ($0.2\%$) were further lost to cloning failures, and $236{,}449$ merge SHAs across $88{,}339$ PRs were unrecoverable for the same reason. We have no evidence of a systematic directional bias arising from these losses.

\textbf{Conclusion validity.} Conflict chunks exhibit intra-merge and intra-repository dependence, so $p$-values are anti-conservative (i.e., Type~I error may be inflated). We therefore emphasize effect sizes (Cram\'{e}r's~$V$ and Cliff's~$\delta$) and do not treat $p{<}0.05$ alone as evidence of practical importance. A more subtle threat is the differential Imprecise rate across resolver types: $16.7\%$ for agent-resolved chunks versus $40.0\%$ for human-resolved chunks, with two components: \emph{localization failure} ($16.1\%$ vs.\ $33.4\%$) and \emph{Postponed} cases ($0.64\%$ vs.\ $6.56\%$, Section~\ref{sec:rq2-postponed}). The higher localization success for agents is plausibly linked to their higher $V_1$ rate, since $V_1$ resolutions exactly match one parent and are easier to anchor. This differential may inflate the agent's apparent $V_1$ dominance; however, the sensitivity analysis in Section~\ref{sec:meth-resolution}, which reassigns Imprecise chunks under worst-case scenarios, bounds the agent-to-human $V_1$ ratio at $1.24{\times}$--$2.45{\times}$, indicating that the observed per-agent contrasts are not artefacts of differential localization.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented the first large-scale empirical study of merge conflicts in AI-authored pull requests that attributes each resolution to a specific coding agent, recovering $50{,}700$ internal merges (of which $14{,}960$ conflicting) across $4{,}806$ repositories from a $210{,}902$-PR scope. Three findings carry the most operational weight. First, conflict resolution in agent-authored PRs is overwhelmingly human-led ($96.1\%$ of conflicting merges), and the per-agent self-resolution rate spans more than $60{\times}$, justifying per-vendor rather than one-size-fits-all review and CI policies. Second, the five agents do not share a common resolution strategy: per-agent contrasts against the same human reference range over an order of magnitude in Cram\'{e}r's~$V$, and Cursor vs.\ Devin is the only indistinguishable pair among the ten. Third, two of the five agents are dominated by a single repository: $55$ of the $61$ Codex self-resolved merges are version-bump patterns from \texttt{BlazingSu/nocobase}, and $10$ of the $12$ Claude Code self-resolved merges come from \texttt{itdojp/ITDO\_ERP2}. For these two agents, the per-agent strategy distribution reflects the dominant repository's workflow, not the agent itself, illustrating that single-repository concentration can drive corpus-wide effect sizes in AI-agent attribution studies.


As future work, a longitudinal analysis would establish whether the per-agent profiles and the single-repository concentration patterns documented here are stable as agent populations and underlying models evolve. A semantic correctness assessment linking each resolution to downstream test outcomes would quantify the practical cost of the per-agent strategy choices we report. The labelled corpus released with this study can also serve as a stratified training set for specialised agentic merge-resolution models that generalise across agents.
